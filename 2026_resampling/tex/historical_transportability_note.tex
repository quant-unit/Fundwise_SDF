{\Large\bfseries How Much History Should Price Private Equity?\par}
{\normalsize Historical relevance in cash-flow discounting\par}
\today

\begin{abstract}
How much historical data should be used to price a newly raised private-equity
fund? Older vintages increase precision but may reflect different financing,
valuation, and exit conditions, while recent vintages remain partly
unrealized. We estimate the same cash-flow discounting model using expanding,
time-decayed, and state-weighted historical samples. In a
vintage-at-formation backtest, each estimator uses only information available
when the target vintage is raised and is evaluated once, after that vintage
matures, on its cash-flow NPV error. We also test whether NAV-based moments
from still-active funds add to, or can replace, realized-cash-flow
information. All choices are judged on the same later cash-flow target. \tbd{State the headline
result in NPV cents per dollar of commitment.} The analysis treats historical
relevance, rather than the number of resampled funds, as the scarce empirical
object.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

How much historical private-capital data should be used to estimate an SDF
for a fund raised today? If the pricing relation is stable, older vintages add
information and full-history estimates become more precise. If it changes,
the same estimator may become precisely estimated for the wrong historical
mixture. More funds within a vintage can measure that vintage more accurately,
but they cannot create additional economic histories.

Private equity makes this bias--variance problem unusually sharp. A long
database contains only a few dozen vintage years; adjacent vintages share much
of the same calendar history; and the vintages closest to the current state
have not yet realized. Reaching backward increases precision but strengthens
the assumption that old pricing relations remain relevant. Requiring cash-flow
realization moves the usable sample away from the present. The estimation
choice therefore runs along two dimensions: which history to use, and which
measurements --- realized cash flows are reliable but old, NAV marks are
current but appraised. Resampling funds resolves neither problem.

The private-equity pricing literature has developed several ways to recover
risk and performance from irregular, non-traded cash flows.
\citet{DriessenLinPhalippou2012} estimate factor exposures and abnormal
performance directly from fund cash flows --- the discounting framework our
estimator adopts throughout --- while
\citet{AngChenGoetzmannPhalippou2018} recover a time series of private-equity
returns from limited-partner cash flows. \citet{KortewegNagel2016} use a
generalized public-market-equivalent approach to risk-adjust venture-capital
cash flows. More recent work refines cash-flow benchmarking and SDF-based
performance measurement \citep{GredilGriffithsStucke2023,KortewegNagel2025}.
These approaches differ in their pricing restrictions and treatment of
illiquidity, but they largely take the historical estimation sample as given.
We instead treat the use of history as the empirical choice. We call a pricing
rule \emph{historically transportable} when a rule estimated on one set of
vintages also prices vintages raised at other times. Transport failure may
reflect smooth drift, breaks, changes in economic-state support, or changes in
fund composition. With so few vintages, the paper does not attempt an omnibus
test that distinguishes all of these mechanisms. It asks which simple use of
history performs best on later cash flows, following the forecasting-under-
instability logic of local predictive evaluation
\citep{PesaranTimmermann2007,Rossi2021}.

The headline design is a vintage-at-formation backtest. For each scoreable
target vintage, we estimate one fixed cash-flow SDF using only information
available immediately before that vintage is raised. We compare an expanding
history, exponential time decay, and time decay augmented by similarity in
credit spreads, interest rates, and public-market valuations. When the target
vintage later matures, each design is scored once on its realized cash-flow
NPV error. This produces one genuine forecast per target vintage and directly
matches the decision faced for a newly raised fund.

The same backtest prices the second dimension. At each formation date, earlier
vintages that are still active contain recent but appraisal-based NAV returns.
A hybrid estimator adds those short-horizon Euler moments to the cash-flow
moments; a NAV-only estimator, the recency endpoint, discards realized cash
flows entirely and estimates from recent marks alone. Together with the
cash-flow-only rules this yields seven headline designs, bracketed by two
bookends: expanding cash-flow history --- maximal history, zero recency ---
and rolling NAV marks --- zero history, maximal recency. Every design must
prove itself on the target vintage's later cash flows; NAV information is
never judged by its ability to fit NAVs.

The paper makes two contributions. First, it provides direct evidence on how

from a longer sample from the relevance gain of a local one. Second, it gives
an appraisal-free test of whether information from still-active funds improves

if expanding history wins: that result
would show that the variance cost of localization dominates any detectable
historical drift in this sample.

\section{Pricing Setup}

\subsection{Pricing Moments}
\label{sec:moments}

The headline analysis fixes one SDF throughout so that differences in loss
identify differences in the use of history rather than differences in factor
specification. Following \citet{DriessenLinPhalippou2012}, the one-period
discount factor is the inverse gross return of a levered public benchmark,
\[
M_{t-1,t}(\beta)
=
\frac{1}{1+r_{f,t}+\beta\,(R_{m,t}-r_{f,t})},
\]
with $\beta\in\Theta$. Setting $\beta=0$ prices the
risk-free asset and $\beta=1$ a self-financing public-market investment
exactly, period by period: benchmark pricing is built into the functional
form rather than imposed through auxiliary moments, and no separate
price-of-risk parameter exists. The abnormal-return intercept of the
original method is fixed at zero; Section~\ref{sec:pricinglaw} states the
resulting estimand. Multifactor SDFs are robustness exercises, not additional
headline designs.\footnote{$\Theta$ bounds $\beta$ so that the levered
benchmark return exceeds $-100\%$ in every sample quarter, keeping all
discount factors positive.} Let
$\Psi_{0,t}(\beta)=\prod_{s=1}^{t}M_{s-1,s}(\beta)$ denote the realized
discount factor from fund inception to quarter $t$, with $\Psi_{0,0}=1$, so
that the time-zero capital call enters undiscounted by construction.

\subsubsection{Seasoned vintages}

For seasoned fund $i$ of vintage $v$ with committed capital $K_i$, net dated
cash flows $CF_{t,i}$ (calls negative, distributions positive), and last
observed cash flow at $T_i$, the realized pricing error per committed dollar
is
\[
g_i^S(\beta)
=
\frac{1}{K_i}\sum_{t=0}^{T_i}\Psi_{0,t}(\beta)\,CF_{t,i},
\]
and the seasoned vintage moment is the equal-weighted fund mean
$\epsilon_v^S(\beta)=N_v^{-1}\sum_{i\in v}g_i^S(\beta)$, where $N_v$ is the
number of admissible funds in vintage $v$; commitment-weighted vintage means
are a pre-specified robustness variant.

The same object defines the paper's common evaluation currency. The realized
evaluation loss of a parameter $\beta$ on a seasoned vintage $v$ is
\[
\widehat U_v^{\mathrm{real}}(\beta)=\epsilon_v^S(\beta)^2,
\]
the squared vintage-mean realized pricing error. Squaring the vintage mean,
rather than averaging squared fund-level errors, targets systematic
mispricing and prevents idiosyncratic fund noise --- which no SDF can price
--- from dominating design comparisons; absolute rather than squared vintage
errors are a robustness check. Because $\Psi_{0,t}$ compounds many quarterly
SDF realizations, a small number of extreme discount-factor paths can
dominate both estimation and evaluation. We therefore report the
concentration of loss contributions across vintages and a winsorized-loss
robustness variant.

\subsubsection{Unseasoned vintages}

For an unseasoned fund, the full NPV moment is unavailable without valuing the
residual claim. The baseline bridge uses the short-horizon NAV Euler error
\[
e^H_{t,i}(\beta)
=
M_{t-1,t}(\beta)R^H_{t,i}-1,
\]
where $R^H_{t,i}$ is the quarterly Horizon-IRR return accounting for interim
calls and distributions. The unseasoned vintage moment scales the pooled
fund-quarter mean into the units of the seasoned block:
\[
\epsilon_v^U(\beta)
=
\bar D\cdot
\frac{1}{n_v^U}\sum_{(i,t)\in v}e^H_{t,i}(\beta),
\]
where $n_v^U$ is the number of admissible fund-quarters of vintage $v$ and
$\bar D$ is the median discounted duration, in quarters, of seasoned funds in
the estimation sample. Duration scaling is only a first-order unit conversion;
it does not make the empirical scales of the two moment blocks comparable. The
objective therefore applies a relative scale weight $\rho_T$ to the
unseasoned block. At each origin, $\rho_T$ is calibrated using only older
vintages that are already seasoned: their early NAV histories are treated as
pseudo-young observations and compared with their full-life NPV moments. The
calibration rule is fixed before forward loss is inspected; $\rho_T=1$ and a
grid around the calibrated value are robustness checks. Euler errors are
never discounted back to inception and never enter the comparison score.
Reported NAVs are smoothed appraisals, so this block is a candidate
information bridge rather than a traded-return restriction that must hold
exactly \citep{brown2023nowcasting}. Smoothing attenuates the covariance
between NAV returns and the benchmark, so a marks-based $\beta$ is identified
largely by the average NAV excess return relative to the realized premium in
the estimation window. This is a property the backtest evaluates, not one we
correct by assumption; unsmoothed returns are a robustness variant.

The main information-set comparison is deliberately parsimonious:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \emph{Exclude}: estimate only with seasoned realized-cash-flow moments;
\item \emph{Hybrid}: add short-horizon NAV Euler moments for unseasoned
      vintages;
\item \emph{NAV-only}: estimate from the Euler moments alone, discarding
      realized cash flows --- the recency endpoint, and the design closest to
      estimating $\beta$ from recent NAV returns in practice.
\end{enumerate}
All three are evaluated on the identical subsequently realized cash flows. A
more complicated measurement model earns a place in the main design only if
these simple comparisons demonstrate economically meaningful value from recent
NAV information.

\subsection{Historical Weights}

At target date $T$, let $z_v$ collect the economic state associated with
vintage $v$. The baseline state vector contains three pre-performance
variables: a credit spread, the interest-rate level, and a public-market
valuation ratio. The variables are measured at or immediately before vintage
formation and standardized using the estimation sample only. State
conditioning also serves as a reduced-form hedge against time-varying premia
on factors the benchmark omits: it localizes estimation in states where such
premia are plausibly similar (Appendix~\ref{app:structural}).

For historical vintage $v$, define the unnormalized relevance weight
\[
w_{v,T}(h,b)
=
\exp\!\left[-\log(2)\frac{T-v}{h}\right]
\exp\!\left[-\frac{1}{2b^2}
  (z_v-z_T)'(z_v-z_T)\right],
\]
where $h$ is a time half-life and $b$ is a common state-similarity bandwidth.
The common bandwidth avoids estimating three separate state sensitivities
from a few dozen vintages. Setting $h=\infty$ removes time decay and setting
$b=\infty$ removes state conditioning.

Let $q_v$ be a reliability weight, set to the vintage fund count $N_v$ in the
baseline; vintage-equal weighting ($q_v=1$) is a pre-specified robustness
variant. The products $w_{v,T}q_v$ are normalized to sum to one jointly
across all vintages entering the objective, seasoned and unseasoned alike, so
that a vintage's relevance does not depend on the block through which it
enters. For historical weighting rule $W$ and information set
$A\in\{\mathrm{Exclude},\mathrm{Hybrid},\mathrm{NAV}\}$, estimate
\[
\widehat\beta_T(W,A)
=
\arg\min_{\beta\in\Theta}
\left\{
\mathbf 1\{A\neq\mathrm{NAV}\}
\sum_{v\in\mathcal V_T^S}\widetilde w_{v,T}
  \epsilon_v^S(\beta)^2
+
\rho_T^A
\sum_{v\in\mathcal V_T^U}\widetilde w_{v,T}
  \epsilon_v^U(\beta)^2
\right\},
\]
where $\rho_T^A$ is $0$ under Exclude, the calibrated $\rho_T$ under Hybrid,
and $1$ under NAV-only, whose single-block objective makes the scale
irrelevant. We collect a complete headline design in $c=(W,A)$ and write
$\widehat\beta_T(c)$ below. Because unseasoned vintages are the most recent,
time decay concentrates weight on the Euler block. Every hybrid configuration
therefore reports its effective Euler share --- the scale-adjusted weight
share
$\pi_U(T)=\rho_T\widetilde W_U/(\widetilde W_S+\rho_T\widetilde W_U)$ with
$\widetilde W_B=\sum_{v\in\mathcal V_T^B}\widetilde w_{v,T}$ --- which makes
this interaction visible rather than leaving it implicit in the kernel and
the scale weight.

The seven headline designs combine three historical weighting rules with the
three information sets:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Expanding}: equal weight on the full available history;
\item \textbf{Decay}: exponential time weights with a fixed baseline
      half-life of 8 vintage years, roughly half a typical fund life;
\item \textbf{Time--state}: the same decay combined with similarity in
      credit spreads, interest rates, and public valuations.
\end{enumerate}
Exclude and Hybrid are each crossed with all three weighting rules; NAV-only
enters once, under the decay weights, so that its comparison with the
corresponding cash-flow rules isolates the information set rather than the
window shape.

Rolling windows, alternative half-lives and bandwidths, NAV-only under
time--state weights or a hard trailing window, multifactor SDFs, and a
regularized time-varying parameter path are robustness exercises
\citep{CuiFengHong2024}. They do not enlarge the headline selection set.

Every candidate must satisfy two feasibility restrictions before it can be
scored: the weighted moment Jacobian must be sufficiently well conditioned,
and the weight concentration must leave an effective vintage count
\[
V_{\mathrm{eff}}(T)=\frac{1}{\sum_v\widetilde w_{v,T}^2}
\]
comfortably above the number of SDF parameters. The count is computed both
over all vintages and, with weights renormalized within the block, over the
seasoned block alone; whenever a design uses cash-flow moments, the threshold
must hold for the seasoned block, since realized cash flows anchor
identification. NAV-only designs face an analogous screen: a minimum
effective number of fund-quarters and a minimum realized variation of the
benchmark excess return within the weighted window, because a window whose
realized premium is close to zero identifies $\beta$ only through noise in
smoothed marks (Appendix~\ref{app:design}). These screens prevent an
apparently local estimator from being driven by one historically similar
vintage, propped up by appraisal-based moments alone, or fitted to a window
without price variation.

\subsubsection{Pricing law versus performance measurement}
\label{sec:pricinglaw}

The fitted $\beta$ is a predictive discount-rule parameter, not a pure risk
exposure or an estimate of abnormal performance. With the intercept fixed at
zero, average in-window performance and unspanned premia can be absorbed into
the fitted exposure. We therefore interpret $\widehat\beta$ only as the
leverage on the public benchmark that best rationalizes the chosen historical
sample. In-window residuals are diagnostics of fit; all performance statements
in the paper concern target vintages excluded from estimation. An appendix
derives the first-order premium channel and reports it as a specification
diagnostic, not as a recovery of structural $\alpha$ and $\beta$.

\subsection{Historical Transportability}

\subsubsection{The transportability matrix}

Let $s$ index a historical estimation window and $t$ an excluded, fully
realized evaluation vintage. Throughout this subsection $s$ denotes the
window's information end date; where present, the time-decay and state
kernels are centered on $s$ and $z_s$ rather than on a target vintage, and
each row uses a single information set,
$A\in\{\mathrm{Exclude},\mathrm{NAV}\}$. Define the NAV-free transport loss
\[
L_{s\rightarrow t}(c)
=
\widehat U_t^{\mathrm{real}}\!\left(
  \widehat\beta_s(c)
\right),
\]
where the evaluation score uses only realized fund cash flows. Omitting the
hybrid keeps every row's information set pure: cash-flow-estimated and
marks-estimated windows can be compared directly. Stacking these losses over
estimation windows and evaluation vintages yields a
\emph{historical transportability matrix}. Its rows are window SDFs, its
columns are evaluation vintages, and each entry measures how well a pricing
law travels across economic time; the NAV rows show how far marks-estimated
rules travel into realized cash flows, the descriptive counterpart of the
paper's fourth question.

Aggregating by the signed temporal distance $d=t-s$, measured from the window
end, gives the transport loss curve
\[
\mathcal T_c(d)
=
\frac{1}{N_d}\sum_s L_{s\rightarrow s+d}(c).
\]
Positive distances measure forward transportability, the only direction with
a real-time interpretation; backward entries are diagnostics of structural
stability, not feasible investment rules. The matrix is descriptive: a flat
surface is consistent with broad transportability, loss increasing in $|d|$
with temporal localization, and isolated high-loss columns with historically
unusual target periods. None of these patterns alone proves a particular
structural-break model.

\subsubsection{Calendar-time overlap}

Adjacent vintage moments share public-factor histories because their funds
remain active for many years. We therefore measure pairwise calendar-time
overlap rather than treating vintage labels as independent by construction.
Let $a_{v,t}$ denote vintage $v$'s absolute discounted cash-flow exposure to
calendar period $t$, evaluated at reference parameter values fixed in
advance.\footnote{A Jacobian-based exposure measure yields an alternative
overlap matrix and is reported as a variant.} Define
\[
O_{vv'}
=
\frac{\sum_t a_{v,t}a_{v',t}}
{\sqrt{\sum_ta_{v,t}^2}\sqrt{\sum_ta_{v',t}^2}}.
\]
The overlap matrix is reported alongside the transportability matrix.
Estimation--evaluation pairs with overlap above a pre-specified threshold are
flagged, and headline comparisons are re-reported excluding them. Because
complete separation would eliminate most of the data, overlap is quantified
and stress-tested rather than treated as a binary admissibility standard.

\subsection{The Vintage-at-Formation Backtest}

Each scoreable target vintage $v$ receives one forecast. Let $\tau_v$ be the
last information date before vintage $v$ is formed. The estimator
$\widehat\beta_v(c)$ uses only cash flows, NAVs, public factors, and state
variables dated no later than $\tau_v$; its time and state kernels are centered
on $v$ and $z_v$. The target vintage never enters its own estimator. Under the
hybrid and NAV-only treatments, only earlier vintages that were still active
at $\tau_v$ contribute NAV Euler moments.

After target vintage $v$ becomes eligible for cash-flow-only evaluation, its
forward loss is
\[
L_v(c)
=
\widehat U_v^{\mathrm{real}}\!\left(\widehat\beta_v(c)\right).
\]
The rule is therefore estimated at the same point at which it would have been
used and the target is scored exactly once. This eliminates repeated use of
the same eventual outcome across adjacent origins and directly matches the
current application: estimate a pricing rule immediately before a new vintage
is raised.

\subsection{What the Backtest Can Establish}

The vintage-at-formation exercise is pseudo-real-time rather than literal
real time. The final extract does not reconstruct historical database
coverage, later backfills, or every field's original reporting date. We use
timestamps where available and exclude suspiciously late first reports, but
the remaining limitation is stated directly.

Maturity creates a second limit: the last scoreable target vintage precedes
the final sample date by roughly one fund life. Applying the selected rule to
today's vintage therefore remains an extrapolation beyond the last directly
validated cohort. We report the last scoreable vintage and whether the current
state lies inside the historical support used by the state kernel.

\subsection{Forward Selection Criterion}
\label{sec:forward}

Let $\mathcal V^{\mathrm{eval}}$ denote the target vintages that can be
estimated at formation and subsequently evaluated on the common cash-flow-only
criterion. For headline design $c=(W,A)$, define
\[
\widehat R(c)
=
\frac{1}{|\mathcal V^{\mathrm{eval}}|}
\sum_{v\in\mathcal V^{\mathrm{eval}}}L_v(c).
\]
All seven designs face the same target vintages, so comparisons are paired.
Fixed-design losses answer the four substantive questions: whether decay
beats expanding history, whether state similarity improves on decay, whether
the NAV bridge improves on the corresponding cash-flow-only rule, and whether
NAV moments alone --- the recency endpoint --- can replace realized cash
flows entirely.

Selecting the lowest loss from the same backtest creates a winner's curse.
The preferred current rule is therefore the simplest design within one
standard error of the minimum and within the model confidence set
\citep{HansenLundeNason2011}. We also evaluate the selection rule sequentially.
At formation of target vintage $v$, selection may use only earlier target
vintages whose evaluation outcomes would already have been observable at
$\tau_v$. The resulting sequence measures what the complete rule, including
selection, would have delivered historically. Fixed-design comparisons and
sequential selection are reported together; neither is described as a clean
estimate of the other's performance.

\subsection{Inference and Reporting}

Model comparisons use target-vintage paired loss differences
\citep{GiacominiWhite2006}. We report root-mean-squared NPV error in cents per
dollar of commitment, the percentage change relative to expanding history,
and the signed errors behind the loss. Inference uses calendar-time blocks that
respect overlapping fund lives, supplemented by leave-one-target-vintage-out
stability. The number of target vintages and funds is shown beside every
comparison. With only a few economic histories, uncertainty intervals and the
model confidence set measure precision; they are not treated as a binary test
of structural stability.

\subsection{Private-Capital Cash-Flow Data}

The fund data are drawn from \tbd{data provider and extraction date}. The unit
of observation is a dated limited-partner cash flow for a \tbd{buyout / broader
private-capital} fund. Required fields are fund identifier, vintage year,

\subsection{The Cash-Flow-Only Evaluation Sample}

Estimation and evaluation use different strictness. The evaluation target uses
only funds recorded as liquidated with zero residual NAV: their loss contains
calls and distributions only, and neither reported NAV nor an estimated
terminal value enters the score. The seasoned cash-flow moments used in
estimation instead admit \emph{near-realized} funds, those whose residual NAV
per dollar of commitment satisfies
\[
r_{i,\tau}=\frac{\mathrm{NAV}_{i,\tau}}{K_i}\leq\delta_K,
\]
with a pre-specified small threshold \tbd{proposed baseline: 5\%}. Unlike the
residual share of total value, this rule directly bounds the omitted appraisal
per dollar of commitment, and it moves the freshest usable estimation vintage
several years closer to each formation date. The bounded truncation reaches
every design through the same funds and largely cancels in paired
comparisons. Liquidated-only estimation and a near-realized evaluation
expansion are robustness variants, each labelled as such and never described
as fully realized.

A fund excluded from evaluation may still contribute to the Euler block of
the hybrid and NAV-only designs at an earlier origin. For every target
vintage we report the available seasoned and unseasoned funds, the number of
liquidated evaluation funds, and the share of vintage commitment represented
by the cash-flow-only target. This makes early-liquidator selection and
coverage differences visible. The descriptive feasibility audit reports the
number of scoreable target vintages under each sample rule before the design
grid is frozen.

\subsection{Public Factors and the Baseline SDF}

Public-market returns and the risk-free rate are obtained from \tbd{source} at
the same frequency as the cash-flow discounting exercise. The headline design
uses the freely estimated one-factor $\beta$ of Section~\ref{sec:moments} for
all seven candidates. Risk-free discounting, the $\beta=1$
public-market-equivalent benchmark, and a low-dimensional multifactor model are
reported as robustness checks. Factor definitions, currency, return convention,
and the mapping from quarterly factors to dated cash flows are fixed before
forward pricing losses are inspected.

\subsection{Economic-State Variables}

The state vector $z_v$ contains one variable for each of three economic
dimensions: a corporate credit spread, the interest-rate level, and a public-
equity valuation ratio. The proposed baseline definitions are the high-yield
option-adjusted spread, a long-term nominal government yield, and the cyclically
adjusted price--earnings ratio \tbd{or a price--dividend ratio}. To avoid using
fund outcomes to define similarity, each variable is measured as its average
over the 12 months ending immediately before the vintage year. At each origin,
levels are standardized using only the information set at formation. We report
whether each target state $z_v$, and the final current state, lies inside the
available historical support. A target outside that support is an extrapolation
problem and should not receive a falsely precise local estimate.

\subsection{Target-Vintage Information Sets}

Target vintages begin with the first formation date at which all seven
headline designs satisfy the effective-vintage, Jacobian, and NAV-window
conditions. For target
vintage $v$, every input is truncated at $\tau_v$, immediately before
formation. The target's pre-formation state $z_v$ is observable, but none of
its fund outcomes are. The same target vintages and evaluation funds are used
for every paired comparison. Sequential selection may use only evaluation
outcomes that would already have become observable by $\tau_v$.

\section{Empirical Results}

\section{Conclusion}

Private-capital SDF estimation cannot solve a thin and delayed history by
resampling funds more aggressively. The economically relevant decision is how
much old history to use for a newly raised vintage and whether current but
appraised NAV information should supplement --- or replace --- realized cash
flows. The vintage-at-formation backtest answers these questions on later
cash flows, with one prediction per target vintage, one fixed baseline SDF,
and two bookends: expanding cash-flow history and rolling NAV marks.

relevance gain from recent or economically similar vintages exceeds its
precision cost. If expanding history remains competitive, the conclusion is
equally useful: the available sample is too thin for more adaptive estimators
to improve reliably. In either case, the current estimate is presented with
the historical vintages that support it and the distance from the last cohort
on which the rule could be directly validated.

\section{NAV-Block Scale Calibration}

The baseline duration scaling puts quarterly Euler errors approximately in
lifetime-NPV units, while $\rho_T$ normalizes their empirical dispersion. At
origin $T$, let $\mathcal P_T$ contain only vintages that are already
liquidated. For each $u\in\mathcal P_T$, reconstruct the NAV Euler moment that
would have been observed after a fixed early-life horizon, and denote its
duration-scaled value at the reference $\beta=1$ by $x_u$. Let $y_u$ be the
corresponding full-life cash-flow NPV moment at the same reference parameter.
Using robust cross-vintage variance estimates computed only within
$\mathcal P_T$, set
\[
\rho_T=\frac{\widehat{\operatorname{Var}}_T(y_u)}
              {\widehat{\operatorname{Var}}_T(x_u)}.
\]
The numerator and denominator are floored away from zero, and the allowable
range of $\rho_T$ is fixed before forward loss is inspected. This calibration
equalizes the historical dispersion of the two blocks; it is not an estimate
of structural measurement precision, does not treat NAV returns as traded
returns, and does not use the target vintage's eventual outcome.

\section{Extended Design Set}
\label{app:design}

The appendix sensitivity set adds rolling windows of 8, 12, and 16 vintages;
time half-lives of 4 and 12 years around the 8-year baseline; state
bandwidths of 0.75 and 3 around the 1.5-unit baseline; and NAV-only variants
under time--state weights, a hard trailing five-year window, and unsmoothed
NAV returns. It also reports risk-free discounting, the $\beta=1$ PME
benchmark, and a low-dimensional multifactor SDF. These variants are compared
with the headline designs but do not enter the headline seven-model selection
set.

The NAV-window screen requires, within the weighted estimation window, a
minimum effective number of fund-quarters,
$n_{\mathrm{eff}}=1/\sum_{(i,t)}\widetilde\omega_{i,t}^2$ computed from the
normalized fund-quarter weights, and a minimum weighted variance of the
benchmark excess return. Both thresholds are fixed in the descriptive
feasibility audit before any forward loss is inspected. Windows failing the
screen are reported as infeasible rather than estimated, since a window in
which the realized premium is close to zero identifies $\beta$ only through
noise in smoothed marks.

As a higher-dimensional check, the regularized time-varying path solves
\[
\min_{\{\beta_v\}}
\sum_v\epsilon_v(\beta_v)^2
+\kappa\sum_{v>1}(\beta_v-\beta_{v-1})^2.
\]
At each sequential target vintage, $\kappa$ may be chosen only from evaluation
outcomes already observable by that vintage's formation date. The method is
reported as a check on whether simple weighting misses economically important
smooth drift, not as an additional headline candidate.

\section{Structural Interpretation Diagnostic}
\label{app:structural}

Fixing the abnormal-return intercept at zero makes the fitted exposure a
composite. In a first-order return representation with true abnormal component
$\alpha$, exposure $\beta$, and included benchmark premium $\lambda_s$ in
estimation window $s$, the zero-intercept fit satisfies
\[
\beta_s^*\approx\beta+\frac{\alpha}{\lambda_s}.
\]
For an evaluation window $t$, the corresponding signed residual is
approximately
\[
\alpha\left(1-\frac{\lambda_t}{\lambda_s}\right).
\]
Thus, even a stable $\alpha$ can generate transport loss when included-factor
premia differ across windows. Omitted unspanned premia create the same
observational problem. Plots of signed residuals against realized benchmark
premia are therefore specification diagnostics only: they cannot by themselves
separate skill, omitted factors, and changes in the pricing relation.
