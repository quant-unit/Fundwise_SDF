**Referee report on “Semiparametric SDF Estimators for Pooled, Non-Traded Cash Flows”**

**Recommendation:** Major revision; not yet ready for acceptance at a top finance journal in its current form.

This is a serious and thoughtful paper. Its core contribution is methodological: it generalizes inception-only cash-flow-based SDF estimation to horizon-averaged criteria, formalizes the target-shift induced by post-inception discounting via a timing-risk-premium wedge, and develops an increasing-domain asymptotic framework for overlapping private-equity cash flows. The paper also includes a substantial simulation section and an empirical application to North American USD buyout and venture capital funds from Preqin. The main empirical message is that single-factor market specifications are much more stable than alpha-augmented or two-factor alternatives, especially once young NAV-heavy vintages are excluded. 

I found the paper interesting and potentially publishable. The strongest part is the conceptual clarification that horizon averaging can improve finite-sample behavior while changing the population target. That is a useful and, to my knowledge, underdeveloped point in this literature. The weakest part is that the paper does not yet fully resolve the tension that this insight creates for empirical interpretation: the estimates the paper prefers in practice are generally not estimates of the structural parameter the theory starts from. The empirical section is informative, but at present it feels more like an illustration of estimator fragility than a fully convincing asset-pricing application.

My overall view is that the paper has a real contribution, but it needs sharper positioning, a more disciplined treatment of the estimand under horizon averaging, and stronger support for the empirical implementation choices.

## Major comments

**1. The paper’s central innovation also creates its main identification problem, and the draft does not yet fully solve it.**
The paper is admirably candid that when (H>0), the estimator targets a horizon-specific pseudo-true parameter (\theta_H^\dagger), not the structural parameter (\theta_0). This is stated clearly in Section 3 and Appendix A. But the empirical narrative still tends to treat medium- and long-horizon estimates as if they were economically interpretable “betas” in the usual structural sense, even though the paper’s own theory says these are criterion-dependent objects contaminated by the timing wedge. If the preferred empirical horizons are around 12.5–15 years because they stabilize finite-sample behavior, then the paper needs a much sharper interpretation section explaining exactly what these estimates mean economically and how readers should compare them to inception-based estimates. Right now, the paper oscillates between a structural and a regularization interpretation.

What I would want in revision is one of two things. Either:
(a) make the paper explicitly about estimation of a family of horizon-indexed objects, with (\theta_0) as a benchmark and (\theta_H^\dagger) as deliberate regularized alternatives; or
(b) provide a principled selection rule or correction that links the empirically preferred horizon back to the structural object.
At present, the paper stops halfway: it proves the target shift, then proceeds to favor shifted targets in practice.

**2. The asymptotic theory is elegant, but the empirical implementation leans on it more than the paper’s own evidence justifies.**
The theoretical framework assumes an increasing-domain setup with (V \to \infty), bounded within-vintage counts, bounded lifetimes, NED/mixing conditions, and a diverging SHAC bandwidth sequence. The empirical implementation, however, uses a seasoned sample capped at vintage year 2010, a fixed 12-year Bartlett bandwidth, and only a small number of effective hv-block folds. The paper itself repeatedly notes that asymptotic standard errors are erratic, conservative, and at odds with the cross-validation stability diagnostics. That substantially weakens the force of the asymptotic inference claims in the application.

I do not object to presenting the asymptotic theory. I do object to how strongly the empirical section still relies on it rhetorically. The revision should either downshift the role of formal asymptotic inference in the empirical application or add much stronger finite-sample validation of the SHAC procedure in the exact sample sizes used empirically. At a minimum I would want a robustness section on bandwidth choice, kernel choice, and perhaps a comparison with a block bootstrap or placebo resampling scheme, even if the latter is only heuristic.

**3. The empirical sample restriction based on young-fund NAVs is sensible, but still too ad hoc for a paper making strong empirical claims.**
One of the paper’s more convincing empirical findings is that including recent vintages mechanically inflates estimated market exposure, and that the effect attenuates when final NAVs are haircut by 50 percent. This is valuable evidence. But the move from that exercise to a hard vintage cutoff at 2010 is still fairly ad hoc, and the 50 percent haircut is itself not structurally motivated. Since the paper’s substantive conclusions depend heavily on excluding young funds, I think the treatment of interim NAVs needs to be more systematic.

A stronger revision would show more of the following: liquidated-only subsamples, observed-cash-flow-only exercises, multiple NAV haircut schedules, explicit separation of realized distributions from terminal NAVs, or a simple measurement-error model for reported NAVs. Right now the empirical message is plausible, but not yet pinned down tightly enough for JFQA.

**4. The simulation design is useful, but too stylized to fully validate the empirical guidance the paper draws from it.**
The simulations condition on realized q-factor paths from 1986–2005, use 20 funds for each of 20 vintages, assume 15 deals per fund, and impose a deal structure with one negative investment and one divestment cash flow per deal, plus iid monthly noise. This is a reasonable laboratory for debugging the estimator, but it does not capture several features that matter for the private-equity application: serially correlated shocks, fund-level heterogeneity, smoothed NAVs, multiple partial exits, correlated deal timing within a fund, and dependence structures richer than vintage proximity. The result is that the simulations support the estimator’s mechanics more than they support the empirical design choices.

This matters because the paper uses those simulations to justify three practical choices: vintage-year portfolios, the simple linear SDF, and a preferred horizon near 12.5–15 years. That is too much weight to put on the current DGP. I would strongly encourage at least one richer simulation calibrated to actual private-equity cash-flow features, especially one with interim NAVs and persistence.

**5. The paper will land better if it is framed more clearly as a methodological contribution, not as a broad empirical statement about private-equity factor structure.**
The empirical sections show that single-factor MKT models are relatively stable, while alpha and q-factor extensions are fragile and often display sign reversals across horizons. That is informative. But the paper’s own interpretation is that these failures likely reflect finite-sample identification limits rather than deep evidence against multifactor pricing in private equity. In that sense, the empirical contribution is mostly diagnostic, not definitive. I think the paper should lean into that.

For JFQA, the paper is strongest as: “here is the right way to think about semiparametric SDF estimation with illiquid pooled cash flows, and here is what current PE data can and cannot identify.” It is weaker as: “here are new empirical asset-pricing facts about BO and VC.” I would rewrite the introduction, abstract, and conclusion accordingly.

## Secondary comments

**6. The relation to Korteweg and Nagel is interesting but still somewhat underdeveloped.**
The paper states that the Korteweg-Nagel estimator is “closely related” but not a literal special case because the order of aggregation differs. That is an important point. But the paper would benefit from a more formal side-by-side algebraic comparison, perhaps a proposition showing exactly when the two criteria coincide, approximately coincide, or diverge. The current discussion is directionally clear but not yet fully pinned down. 

**7. Assumption 1 needs more discussion of economic interpretation.**
The deal-level decomposition assumes one negative investment cash flow and a finite stream of positive divestment cash flows per deal. I understand that this is a latent accounting device rather than an observed-data requirement. Still, for private-equity funds with recycling, fees, follow-on investments, write-downs, and intermediate NAV effects, readers will reasonably ask how innocuous this decomposition really is. The paper should do more to explain what is lost and what is without loss of generality. 

**8. The horizon-choice discussion needs an operational rule.**
The paper says the optimal horizon depends on the environment and that shorter-lifetime settings do not admit a simple rule of thumb, while the empirical section nonetheless focuses on horizons around 12.5–15 years. That gap should be closed. Even a pragmatic criterion based on cross-validation or simulated MSE would help.

**9. The cross-validation material is good and should be promoted.**
The hv-block design is one of the most practically useful parts of the paper. In some sense, it is what rescues the empirical section from overinterpreting unstable asymptotic inference. I would elevate it earlier in the paper and give readers a cleaner rule for how to use SDCV and SRCV in model selection.

## What would make me more positive in a revision

A revision would move me materially closer to a favorable recommendation if it did four things:

1. Reframe the paper around horizon-indexed estimands and be explicit that (H>0) targets are regularized, not structural, unless additional assumptions are imposed.
2. Strengthen the empirical treatment of interim NAVs and seasoned-sample selection.
3. Expand the simulation design to include more realistic private-equity cash-flow and dependence features.
4. Tone down the reliance on asymptotic SEs in the application, or add serious finite-sample robustness for inference.

## Bottom line

This is a good paper with a genuine methodological idea. The timing-risk-premium / target-shift insight is the real contribution, and the draft contains enough substance that I would encourage revision rather than dismissal. But for a journal like JFQA, the current version still overreaches a bit on empirical interpretation and underdevelops the operational implications of its own theory. My recommendation would therefore be: promising, but substantial revision required before publication should be considered.
