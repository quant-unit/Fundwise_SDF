## Referee Report (Professional Style)

### Recommendation

**Major Revision (borderline reject in current form for JFQA, but with clear potential).**

### Summary of the paper

The paper proposes a semiparametric **Least-Mean-Distance (LMD)** estimator for SDF pricing of pooled, non-traded cash flows, designed to unify and generalize Driessen et al. (2012) and Korteweg–Nagel (2016). The paper contributes (i) a flexible estimator based on averaging pricing errors over multiple discounting/compounding dates (T_i), (ii) a spatial/NED asymptotic framework with SHAC inference, and (iii) an empirical application to Preqin BO/VC funds using q-factor-style public benchmarks. The headline empirical conclusions are that single-factor market models are robust, while multi-factor and alpha models are fragile, and that a long compounding horizon (around 15 years) materially improves finite-sample behavior.     

This is an important problem and the paper is technically ambitious. I particularly like the effort to formalize the tradeoff between NPV-only pricing and horizon-averaging (your “Timing Risk Premium” framing), and the attempt to connect private-market dependence to spatial econometrics.   

---

## What I think is strong

The paper has several real strengths:

1. **Clear motivation and relevance.** The question—how to do SDF-style performance evaluation with irregular private equity cash flows—remains central in private markets. The paper is well-positioned relative to Driessen et al. and Korteweg–Nagel.   

2. **Unification attempt is valuable.** Framing the estimator as an extremum/LMD problem and showing how Driessen-style inception-only pricing appears as a special case is useful pedagogically and methodologically. Equation (18) is a clean organizing object.  

3. **Theoretical attention to dependence structure.** The move away from ad hoc iid bootstrap to a dependence-aware framework is directionally correct and important for PE/VC cash flows. The paper’s Assumptions 4–5 and SHAC construction are thoughtful.  

4. **Empirical diagnosis of NAV contamination is genuinely useful.** The vintage cutoff / NAV sensitivity analysis (especially the monotone beta drift when unseasoned funds are included) is one of the most compelling practical contributions in the paper.   

---

## Major comments (substantive issues)

### 1) The paper mixes two different estimands, and the inference language does not fully separate them

This is the most important issue.

You correctly show that inception-only discounting (NPV-only) yields the unbiased moment condition under your setup (Assumption 2 / Proposition 1), while including future-value dates introduces a nonzero Timing Risk Premium term.  

But later, the paper uses horizon-averaging (with (H>0)) as a deliberate finite-sample bias correction and still often discusses estimates as if they are estimates of the same structural SDF parameter (\theta_0) (e.g., “true economic factor exposures” and “bias-minimizing horizon”).  

This creates a conceptual gap:

* Under (T_i) containing NFV terms, the estimator is generally converging to a **pseudo-true parameter** (or a horizon-specific target), not necessarily (\theta_0).
* The asymptotic theory in Propositions 2–3 is framed around (\theta_0), and the paper itself notes residual bias may persist when future dates are included.  

**What to fix:**
Reframe the paper around **two estimands**:

* **Structural pricing parameter** (NPV-only, unbiased target under Assumption 2)
* **Finite-sample regularized horizon estimator** (H-averaged, pseudo-true target depending on (H))

Then treat the horizon-averaged estimator explicitly as a **regularized estimator** (or weakly misspecified moment combination), not as a direct estimator of the same object. This would greatly improve clarity and credibility.

---

### 2) The “Timing Risk Premium as bias correction” is interesting, but currently too heuristic for JFQA standards

The paper’s key innovation is that adding future-value dates induces a TRP term that can offset nonlinear finite-sample bias. This is a very interesting idea, and the simulation evidence is provocative.   

However, as currently written:

* The “sweet spot” (12.5–15 years) is mostly simulation-driven and DGP-dependent.
* The simulations condition on a realized factor path (1986–2005), and the paper itself notes this may attenuate TRP covariance terms.  
* The empirical recommendation “use ~15 years” is stated very strongly given the above. 

**What to fix:**
I would strongly recommend a formal section titled something like **“Target parameter under horizon averaging”** and a more explicit **bias-variance-risk decomposition** for the parameter itself (not just pricing errors). Also add a sensitivity exercise showing the “optimal” (H) under multiple DGPs and factor regimes, including different equity premium paths and varying cash-flow timing patterns.

**Our response:**
* We will extend our Appendix Section \section{Bias--Variance Tradeoff of $\mathcal{T}$-Averaging} \label{sec:appendix_bias_variance} to include a more explicit bias-variance-risk decomposition that bridges the gap between theory and practical advices.
---

### 3) hv-block cross-validation is not a substitute for valid statistical inference

This is the second major problem.

You write that you “replace the bootstrap standard error calculation of Driessen et al. (2012) with hv-block cross-validation,” and you report “SECV” and “tCV” as if they are inferential objects.   

But the standard deviation of estimates across CV folds is **not generally a standard error of the full-sample estimator**, and the resulting “tCV” is not a standard test statistic with a known null distribution. This is especially true when folds are overlapping in design logic, selected for dependence reduction, and the number of folds is small and changes with vintage cutoff.  

This does not mean the exercise is useless—it is actually useful as a **stability diagnostic**. But it should be presented as:

* **out-of-sample stability / dispersion**, not inference;
* **model selection support**, not significance testing.

**What to fix:**
Keep hv-block CV, but relabel it as **stability diagnostics**. For inference, consider:

* dependence-robust subsampling / block bootstrap tailored to vintage dependence,
* or report only SHAC inference and explicitly state small-sample caveats,
* or use sample-splitting solely for model selection and a separate inferential step.

This is likely a decisive issue for JFQA referees.

**Our response:**
* We will clarify how hv-block CV should be used as a stability diagnostic in line with the Racine 2000 paper. We will be more precise in our language! We will exapnd on the Racine 2000 paper

### 4) The “generalizes Korteweg–Nagel (2016)” claim is currently overstated

I understand the mathematical point in Proposition 4, but the way it is currently framed risks overclaiming. You “recover” KN by changing the order of summation and loss evaluation, defining a GMM-style loss, and pooling all funds into one unit ((n=1)); however, this is not the same estimator architecture in the practical/economic sense, because KN estimate the SDF on public returns and then value PE cash flows, whereas your estimator estimates directly on PE cash flows.   

That distinction matters economically and statistically (power, benchmark interpretation, and ex-post vs ex-ante conditioning). KN explicitly emphasize sample-period alignment for SDF estimation and benchmarking. 

**What to fix:**
Tone down “nests both estimators” to something like:

* “provides a unifying **objective-function perspective**”
* “recovers Driessen exactly and reproduces key structural features of KN under an alternative ordering/aggregation”

That would be more accurate and less likely to trigger pushback.

**Our response:**
* Agree, we will be more conservative in our language!

### 5) NAV treatment is a major empirical vulnerability, and the current mitigation is too ad hoc

You appropriately highlight the distortion from using latest NAV as terminal cash flow for non-liquidated funds, and the cutoff analysis is excellent.   

However, the main mitigation is:

* a vintage cutoff (2010), and
* a sensitivity with a blanket 50% NAV discount. 

For a JFQA submission, reviewers are likely to ask for something closer to Driessen et al.’s residual value modeling logic (or a modern version), rather than a fixed haircut. Driessen explicitly model final market values of non-liquidated funds conditional on fund characteristics and reporting lags. 

**What to fix:**
At minimum add one principled residual-value adjustment:

* predictive NAV-to-MV calibration (age, last NAV update, last distribution lag, size, strategy, region),
* liquidated-only subsample sanity check,
* and possibly a reporting-lag filter.

This will materially strengthen the empirical credibility.

**Our response:**
* We prefer to keep it simple and add more constant NAV discount values for the final NAVs. To currently 100% and 50%, we can add 75%, 25% and 0% to give results for a range without introducing complex (NAV-discount-approximation) models that are not directly related to the core of the paper.

**Our response:**

If the main qualitative conclusions survive those stress tests, the paper becomes much stronger.

## Minor comments / revision suggestions (important but easier to fix)

1. **Clarify terminology around “unbiased.”** The paper sometimes uses “unconditionally unbiased” (moment level) and “bias-minimizing” (finite sample parameter level). These are not the same and should be kept sharply distinct.  

2. **Separate model selection from inference in notation.** If you keep SECV/tCV, rename them (e.g., “fold dispersion” and “stability ratio”) to avoid inferential overtones. 

3. **Report bandwidth sensitivity.** You fix SHAC bandwidth at 12 years; that needs a sensitivity table (e.g., 6/9/12/15 years) and perhaps alternative kernels.  

4. **Distance metric sensitivity.** Assumption 4 uses vintage-year distance for convenience, but KN motivate overlap-based dependence. Show robustness to a cash-flow-overlap distance measure (at least in a subset).  

5. **Global factor source and currency handling.** Explicitly state currency denomination of Preqin cash flows and FX treatment. The factor source is clear, but the mapping is not. 

6. **Optimization details.** Please report optimizer, starting values, convergence criteria, number of restarts, and handling of boundary solutions. The finite-difference Hessian discussion is useful but insufficient for replication confidence. 

7. **Reproducibility statement is good—surface it earlier.** You mention code/data repository access; moving a brief reproducibility note into the main empirical setup will help. 

8. **Tone down direct claims about Driessen/KN assumptions.** Some comparisons read as dismissive. Emphasize complementary perspectives rather than “superior” framework language.  

9. **Table design / readability.** Horizon-by-horizon tables are rich but heavy. Consider moving some to appendix and foregrounding 3–4 key horizons (0, 10, 15, 20 years) in main text. (The 15-year row is clearly central for your argument, e.g., BO ~0.786/0.799 and VC ~1.263/1.047 under EW/FW.)  

10. **Interpretation of long-horizon attenuation.** The decline in beta with horizon is interesting, but readers will want clearer separation between estimator bias correction vs economic interpretation (e.g., true lower systematic risk vs mechanical regularization effect).

## Bottom line

I think this is a **serious and potentially publishable paper**, but it currently reads like a strong working paper that is trying to do three things at once (new estimator, asymptotic theory, finite-sample regularization, and empirical private equity diagnosis) without fully separating the statistical targets and inferential logic.

If you address the two biggest issues—

1. **recast horizon-averaging as a distinct (regularized/pseudo-true) estimand**, and
2. **stop treating hv-block CV dispersion as standard-error-based inference**—

the paper becomes much more compelling. The NAV diagnostics and the BO/VC horizon evidence are genuinely useful and could make a nice JFQA contribution if the econometric claims are tightened.
