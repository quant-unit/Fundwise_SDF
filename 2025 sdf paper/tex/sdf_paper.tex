\begin{abstract}
	We study identification and semiparametric estimation of stochastic discount factors from pooled non-traded cash flows. 
	The framework generalizes the inception-based approach of \cite{DLP12} and accommodates dependence induced by overlapping cash-flow streams. 
	We show that horizon choice creates a tradeoff between structural fidelity and finite-sample stability: averaging pricing errors over additional compounding dates can materially stabilize estimation, but generally does so by introducing a Compounding-Horizon Wedge that shifts the population pricing restriction. 
	In private-equity cash-flow panels, the framework yields more stable evidence on broad market exposure in seasoned portfolios than on abnormal performance or richer factor decompositions.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Stochastic Discount Factor, Semiparametric Estimation, Spatial Dependence, Generalized Method of Moments (GMM), Private Equity, Illiquid Assets

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

% Option A:
%Do private equity funds deliver abnormal performance, and how should their systematic risk be measured?
%These questions are central to a large and growing literature \citep[see][for comprehensive reviews]{K19,korteweg2023risk}, yet answering them with cash-flow-based methods confronts a fundamental identification challenge that existing work has not fully addressed.

% Option B:
How should stochastic discount factor (SDF) estimators be identified and interpreted when the underlying data consist of pooled, non-traded cash flows rather than traded returns?
This question arises in a growing private-market asset-pricing literature concerned with abnormal performance and systematic risk in settings such as private equity \citep[see][for comprehensive reviews]{K19,korteweg2023risk}.
The semiparametric SDF estimators of \citet{DLP12} and \citet{KN16}, now standard tools in that literature, recover abnormal performance ($\alpha$) and factor exposures ($\beta$) from pooled cash flows without strong distributional assumptions.
But an important practical issue remains unresolved: which economically meaningful objects can those estimators identify with stability in the sparse, overlapping, and partially NAV-based panels that researchers actually observe?

This paper studies that question by formalizing a tradeoff that is implicit in SDF estimation with pooled, non-traded cash flows.
In typical private-capital fund samples, inception-only pricing errors, as in \citet{DLP12}, can be highly unstable because the estimating criterion is nonlinear and the effective cross-sectional sample size is limited.
A natural response is to average pricing errors over additional compounding dates beyond inception.
But this is not merely a statistical refinement: once pricing errors are evaluated at horizons beyond inception, the estimator generally targets a different population object.
We formalize this mechanism as the \emph{Compounding-Horizon Wedge} and show that horizon choice creates a tradeoff between finite-sample stabilization and population target shift.
At the same time, the simulation evidence below shows that this estimand shift need not be purely a cost: over an intermediate range of horizons, horizon averaging can improve finite-sample recoverability not only by lowering dispersion, but also by reducing higher-order small-sample bias of the estimator around its horizon-specific target.

We develop this framework in the context of private-equity cash flows and apply it to Preqin data on 894 Buyout and 931 Venture Capital funds (North America, USD, vintages 1982--2010).
The empirical results deliver three main findings.
First, parsimonious single-factor market models yield comparatively stable estimates across a broad range of compounding horizons, with market betas of roughly 0.8--1.2 for Buyout and 1.0--1.9 for Venture Capital, depending on horizon and weighting scheme.
Second, once an $\alpha$ intercept or a second factor is introduced, estimates become highly unstable: market betas oscillate, sign reversals occur, and asymptotic standard errors become erratic, consistent with weak identification rather than a well-determined multifactor structure.
Third, including unseasoned post-2010 vintages with large reported NAV components mechanically inflates estimated market betas (for Buyout, from about 1.2 to 2.4), underscoring that sample construction is itself an identification choice in private-equity asset pricing.

These findings do not imply that private-equity alpha is zero or that multifactor models are wrong; they indicate that semiparametric alpha estimates from currently available pooled cash-flow panels are weakly identified and highly sensitive to implementation choices.
That qualification matters for interpreting both positive and negative findings on private-equity outperformance across the broader literature.

The paper makes three contributions.

\emph{First,} we characterize identification in semiparametric cash-flow-based SDF estimation under flexible horizon choice.
We derive a generalized pooled-cash-flow pricing framework that makes explicit how the choice of discounting date affects the estimand, nesting the inception-only criterion of \citet{DLP12} as a special case.
This formulation reveals a distinction that, to our knowledge, has not been formalized: a compounding horizon may improve finite-sample performance without preserving the original inception-based pricing restriction.
The framework also clarifies the relation to \citet{KN16}. Their estimator is closely connected but not a literal special case because the order of aggregation differs, so that the two approaches generally converge to different pseudo-true parameter vectors under misspecification.

\emph{Second,} we formulate the estimator as a nonlinear Least-Mean-Distance (LMD) problem for pooled, non-traded cash flows \citep{PP97} and develop an increasing-domain asymptotic framework for inference under dependence induced by overlapping funds and adjacent vintages.
This fills a gap in the existing literature: \citet{DLP12} use within-portfolio infill asymptotics that do not generate new aggregate-state realizations, while \citet{KN16} rely on nonoverlapping payoff windows that are scarce in typical panels.
Our vintage-year increasing-domain device is better aligned with unconditional identification when new vintages bring new macroeconomic realizations.
In the empirical application, however, we treat finite-sample simulation evidence and stability diagnostics as the primary basis for interpretation whenever asymptotic approximations appear unreliable.
In that sense, this is an identification and stability paper for private-market asset pricing.

\emph{Third,} we provide simulation and empirical evidence on the practical boundaries of semiparametric SDF identification for private equity.
We validate the estimator's finite-sample behavior in Monte Carlo experiments calibrated to private-equity-like cash-flow environments and implement $hv$-block cross-validation diagnostics \citep{R00} tailored to overlapping cash-flow dependence.
The empirical application demonstrates that, in our sample, current cash-flow panels support stable inference about broad market exposure in seasoned vintage-year portfolios but not about abnormal performance or richer factor decompositions.

\paragraph{Related literature.}
The paper contributes to the semiparametric private-equity performance literature that builds on \citet{DLP12} and \citet{KN16}.
The \citet{DLP12} estimator has been widely applied to price human capital contracts, venture capital, real estate, and private equity funds \citep{kroencke2013return,kim2015estimation,farrelly2019risk,lee2019new,zhao2020master,TP24}.
The \citet{KN16} framework has found broad application spanning private equity, infrastructure, REITs, CLOs, impact funds, and private debt \citep{giommetti2021risk,GN18,andonov2021institutional,riddiough2022private,boyer2023discount,cordell2023clo,erel2024risk,jeffers2024risk,KN24,korteweg2024private}.
Our LMD framework contributes by providing a loss-function formulation that encompasses \citet{DLP12} and contextualizes \citet{KN16} while supplying rigorous increasing-domain asymptotics (drawing on \cite{PP97}, \cite{KS11}, and \cite{JP12}).
While \citet{GSW20} highlighted that NPV-based measures are biased relative to per-period returns, we explicitly formalize this discrepancy as the Compounding-Horizon Wedge, a concept related to discount-rate risk in secondary markets \citep{boyer2023discount}.

More broadly, the paper connects to parametric private-equity models \citep{FNP12,ACGP18}, the NAV-smoothing literature \citep{BGK19,JLRS20,brown2023nowcasting}, credit-market approaches to private-firm valuation \citep{HSS23}, and the SDF performance evaluation tradition in public equities \citep{FFJT02} and hedge funds \citep{LXZ16}.
From an econometric perspective, the paper is also complementary to \citet{burnside2016identification}, who studies identification and inference in linear SDF models with traded excess returns under alternative normalizations. 
In our pooled non-traded cash-flow setting, the key issue is instead that horizon averaging regularizes the sample criterion while shifting the population pricing restriction.

\subsection{Asset pricing for pooled cash flows}
\label{sec:asset_pricing_pooled}

Because private equity funds invest capital over multiple quarters, any fund-level pricing restriction must aggregate deal-level moments across different investment dates.
This subsection asks under what conditions such a pooled cash-flow stream satisfies a valid stochastic-discount-factor pricing restriction.
The central finding is that the choice of discounting date is not innocuous: discounting at or before the first investment date preserves a clean conditional zero-moment condition (Lemma~\ref{lem:deal_level_timing}, Part~1), whereas post-investment discounting or future-value compounding generally introduces a timing-related covariance wedge (Parts~2 and~3).
The argument proceeds in three steps.
First, we state a deal-level decomposition of fund cash flows (Assumption~\ref{ass:deal_level_dgp}) and derive the timing result at the deal level (Lemma~\ref{lem:deal_level_timing}).
Second, we aggregate to the fund level and show that pooled Net Present Value (NPV) moments satisfy a valid zero conditional moment restriction whenever discounting occurs at or before fund inception (Proposition~\ref{theo:inception_value}).
Third, we discuss the implications for Net Future Value (NFV) moments and the interpretation of alpha measured ex~post.
Throughout, the deal-level structure serves only as a latent accounting device used to illuminate and justify the fund-level restriction; only pooled fund cash flows are required as observable inputs to the estimator.

For fund $i$, let $CF_{i,t}$ denote the observed net cash flow at time $t$, and define the fund inception date by $t_i^0 := \min_j t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}$.
All fund-level variables are constructed from the following deal-level data generation process.

\begin{assume}
	\label{ass:deal_level_dgp}
	\textbf{Deal-level decomposition.}
	For each fund $i$, there exist $J_i$ underlying deals such that:
	\begin{enumerate}
		\item Deal $j$ has exactly one negative investment cash flow $\mathrm{Inv}_{i,j}<0$ at time $t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}\in\{0,1,\dots,T-1\}$.
		\item Deal $j$ has a finite stream of positive divestment cash flows $(\mathrm{Div}_{i,j,k})_{k=1,\dots,K_{i,j}}$ with $\mathrm{Div}_{i,j,k}>0$ and $t_{i,j,k}^{\mathrm{Div}}>t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}$ for all $k$.
		\item Let $cf_{i,j,t}$ denote the deal-level cash flow of deal $j$ in fund $i$ at time $t$. Then the observed fund-level cash flow is the cross-deal aggregate
		\[
		CF_{i,t}=\sum_{j=1}^{J_i} cf_{i,j,t}
		\qquad \forall i,t.
		\]
		\item The fund has no cash flows before inception: $CF_{i,t} = 0$ for all $t < t_i^0$.
	\end{enumerate}
\end{assume}

Under our central asset-pricing restriction, each deal is correctly priced by the stochastic discount factor at its investment date. Formally, the deal-level pricing error satisfies
\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:deal_pricing}
	\mathbb{E} \left[ 
	\delta_{i,j}
	\left| \mathcal{F}_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}} \right.
	\right] = 0
	\quad \forall \ i,j
\end{equation}
where the deal-level pricing error is defined by
\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:deal_pricing_error}
	\delta_{i,j} :=
	\mathrm{Inv}_{i,j} +
	\sum_{k=1}^{K_{i,j}}
	\frac{\Psi_{t_{i,j,k}^{\mathrm{Div}}}}{\Psi_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}}}
	\mathrm{Div}_{i,j,k}
\end{equation}
and the multi-period SDF $\Psi_t = \prod_{u=1}^t M_u$ discounts a time-$t$ cash flow to time 0 (and $\Psi_0 = 1$).

The next result characterizes how this deal-level pricing restriction depends on the choice of discounting or compounding date.

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:deal_level_timing}
Suppose Equation~\eqref{eq:deal_pricing} holds.

\begin{enumerate}
	\item If $\tau \le t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}$, then
	\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:zero_deal_expectation}
	\mathbb{E}\!\left[\frac{\Psi_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}}}{\Psi_\tau}\delta_{i,j}\,\middle|\,\mathcal F_\tau\right]=0.
	\end{equation}

	\item If $\tau > t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}$, then
	\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:non_zero_deal_expectation}
	\mathbb{E}\!\left[\frac{\Psi_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}}}{\Psi_\tau}\delta_{i,j}\,\middle|\,\mathcal F_\tau\right]
	=\frac{\Psi_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}}}{\Psi_\tau}\,
	\mathbb{E}\!\left[\delta_{i,j}\mid\mathcal F_\tau\right],
	\end{equation}
	which need not equal zero.

	\item Under the same pre-investment timing as Part~(1), suppose additionally that the pricing error is compounded forward to a date $t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Future}} > t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}$. If $\tau \leq t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}} < t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Future}}$, then 
	define the NFV-type moment
	\[
	m_{i,j}^{\mathrm{NFV}}(\tau,t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Future}})
	:=
	\frac{\Psi_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Future}}}}{\Psi_\tau}\,\delta_{i,j}.
	\]
	Its expected value
	\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:future_value_bias_derivation}
	\mathbb{E} \left[ m_{i,j}^{\mathrm{NFV}}(\tau,t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Future}}) \mid \mathcal{F}_{\tau} \right] 
	= 
	\mathbb{E} \left[ 
	\frac{\Psi_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}}}{\Psi_{\tau}}
	\mathrm{Cov} \left( \frac{\Psi_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Future}}}}{\Psi_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}}}, \delta_{i,j} \mid \mathcal{F}_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}} \right) \mid \mathcal{F}_{\tau} \right]
	\end{equation}
	generally contains a conditional covariance term and therefore need not equal zero.
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Consider part (1). Applying the law of iterated expectations,\footnote{At the boundary $\tau = t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}$, the SDF ratio $\Psi_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}}/\Psi_{\tau} = 1$ is a trivial constant, so the covariance with $\delta_{i,j}$ is zero and the same argument applies.} 
since $\tau \le t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}$, the SDF ratio is known at $t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}$ and can be factored out yielding
\[
\mathbb{E} \left[ \frac{\Psi_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}}}{\Psi_{\tau}} \delta_{i,j} \mid \mathcal{F}_{\tau} \right] 
= 
\mathbb{E} \left[ \frac{\Psi_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}}}{\Psi_{\tau}} \underbrace{\mathbb{E} \left[ \delta_{i,j} \mid \mathcal{F}_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}} \right]}_{= 0 \text{ by Equation~\eqref{eq:deal_pricing}}} \mid \mathcal{F}_{\tau} \right] 
= 0.
\]

For part (2), since $t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}} < \tau$, the ratio
$\Psi_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}}/\Psi_\tau$ is $\mathcal F_\tau$-measurable, so
\[
\mathbb{E}\!\left[\frac{\Psi_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}}}{\Psi_\tau}\delta_{i,j}\,\middle|\,\mathcal F_\tau\right]
=
\frac{\Psi_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}}}{\Psi_\tau}
\mathbb{E}\!\left[\delta_{i,j}\mid \mathcal F_\tau\right].
\]
Since some divestment cash flows of deal $j$ may have been paid by date $\tau$, $\delta_{i,j}$ is partially $\mathcal{F}_\tau$-measurable, and its conditional expectation given $\mathcal{F}_\tau$ generally reflects realized outcomes rather than the zero-mean restriction at $t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}$.

For part (3), using the law of iterated expectations with an intermediate conditioning on $\mathcal{F}_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}}$,
\begin{align*}
	\mathbb{E} \left[ m_{i,j}^{\mathrm{NFV}}(\tau,t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Future}}) \mid \mathcal{F}_{\tau} \right] 
	&= 
	\mathbb{E} \left[ 
	\frac{\Psi_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}}}{\Psi_{\tau}} 
	\mathbb{E} \left[ \frac{\Psi_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Future}}}}{\Psi_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}}} \delta_{i,j} \mid \mathcal{F}_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}} \right] \mid \mathcal{F}_{\tau} \right] 
		\\
	&= 
	\mathbb{E} \left[ 
	\frac{\Psi_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}}}{\Psi_{\tau}}
	\mathrm{Cov} \left( \frac{\Psi_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Future}}}}{\Psi_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}}}, \delta_{i,j} \mid \mathcal{F}_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}} \right) \mid \mathcal{F}_{\tau} \right]
\end{align*}
where we used the conditional covariance identity and Equation~\eqref{eq:deal_pricing}.
\end{proof}

Part~(3) of Lemma~\ref{lem:deal_level_timing} shows that compounding deal cash flows to a future date generally destroys the zero conditional moment restriction because the resulting expression contains a covariance between the pricing error and future SDF realizations. 
We refer to this non-zero expected value as a \emph{timing-related covariance wedge}. 
Economically, it is the time-$\tau$ price of an investment-date covariance term between continuation SDF realizations and the discounted divestment cash flows over the illiquid holding period. 
At the level of the generalized estimator, this mechanism induces the \textbf{Compounding-Horizon Wedge}: once the criterion averages over post-investment or future-value revaluation dates, the timing-related covariance wedge enters the pooled objective and shifts the population target away from the inception-based benchmark.

To clarify the economic mechanism behind the Compounding-Horizon Wedge, let us briefly analyze Equation~\eqref{eq:future_value_bias_derivation} with only one divestment cash flow per deal ($K_{i,j}=1$).
In this case, the covariance term reads 
\[
\mathrm{Cov} \left( \frac{\Psi_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Future}}}}{\Psi_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}}},\
\mathrm{Inv}_{i,j} +
\frac{\Psi_{t_{i,j,k}^{\mathrm{Div}}}}{\Psi_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}}}
\mathrm{Div}_{i,j,k} \mid \mathcal{F}_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}} \right)
=
\mathrm{Cov} \left( 
\prod_{u=t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}+1}^{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Future}}} M_u, 	\
\mathrm{Div}_{i,j,k} 
\prod_{u=t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}+1}^{t_{i,j,k}^{\mathrm{Div}}} M_u
\mid \mathcal{F}_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}} \right)
\]
Thus, for $K_{i,j}=1$, the covariance is naturally expected to be largest when $t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Future}}$ is equal to $t_{i,j,k}^{\mathrm{Div}}$, since the continuation SDF and the discounted divestment payoff then load on the same SDF path. 
This ranking is not automatic, however, and requires additional restrictions on the post-divestment SDF dynamics (e.g, ${\Psi_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Future}}}} / {\Psi_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Div}}}}$ being conditionally mean-independent of $\mathcal{F}_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Div}}}$ given  $\mathcal{F}_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}}$).

To conclude our analysis of Part 3 of Lemma~\ref{lem:deal_level_timing}, NFV-type moments are inappropriate as primitive ex-ante pricing moments unless the timing-related covariance wedge is modeled explicitly.
They can still be informative for ex-post benchmarking, but the resulting performance measure must then be interpreted as a composite object that mixes true abnormal performance (skill) with systematic exposure to the states in which realization and discounting occur.

\begin{proposition}
	\label{theo:inception_value}
	\textbf{Price of a pooled cash-flow stream at or before fund inception.}
	If $\tau \le t_i^0$, then
	\begin{equation}
		\label{eq:price_pooled_cash_flows}
		\mathbb{E}\!\left[
		\sum_{t=\tau}^{T}\frac{\Psi_t}{\Psi_\tau}CF_{i,t}
		\,\middle|\,\mathcal F_\tau
		\right]=0.
	\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If $\tau\le t_i^0$, then $\tau\le t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}$ for every deal $j$ in fund $i$. Using the deal-level decomposition of the pooled cash-flow stream and Equation~\eqref{eq:deal_pricing_error},
\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:price_pooled_cash_flows_proof_1}
	\mathbb{E}\!\left[
	\sum_{t=\tau}^{T}\frac{\Psi_t}{\Psi_\tau}CF_{i,t}
	\,\middle|\,\mathcal F_\tau
	\right]
	=
	\mathbb{E}\!\left[
	\sum_{j=1}^{J_i}\frac{\Psi_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}}}{\Psi_\tau}\delta_{i,j}
	\,\middle|\,\mathcal F_\tau
	\right].
\end{equation}
Applying Equation~\eqref{eq:zero_deal_expectation} term-by-term yields
\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:price_pooled_cash_flows_proof_2}
	\mathbb{E}\!\left[
	\sum_{j=1}^{J_i}\frac{\Psi_{t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}}}{\Psi_\tau}\delta_{i,j}
	\,\middle|\,\mathcal F_\tau
	\right]
	=0.
\end{equation}
\end{proof}


Proposition~\ref{theo:inception_value} provides the clean benchmark for identification: pooled NPV moments are theoretically well founded when discounting occurs at or before fund inception. Once $\tau>t_i^0$, some underlying deals are active or already partially realized, so the corresponding pooled pricing error incorporates the Compounding-Horizon Wedge.
The choice of discounting dates is therefore not innocuous.
Section~\ref{sec:fundwise_lmd_estimator} formalizes this choice by defining an estimator that operates entirely on pooled fund cash flows (the deal-level decomposition having served its purpose as an analytical device) and explicitly specifies which discounting dates are used.
When it restricts to dates $\tau \le t_i^0$, the pooled fund-level pricing error inherits a valid zero conditional moment.
When it includes dates $\tau > t_i^0$, it generally targets a tilted criterion that embeds the Compounding-Horizon Wedge characterized by Equation \eqref{eq:future_value_bias_derivation}.

Critically, the Compounding-Horizon Wedge is not solely relevant for ex-post benchmarking.
It also fundamentally drives the large-sample and small-sample properties of our generalized LMD estimator. 
As we demonstrate in later sections, averaging pricing errors over extended horizons deliberately introduces wedge-bearing discounting dates that smooth the nonlinear sample criterion and can materially improve finite-sample behavior, even though doing so shifts the population target.



\subsection{Least-Mean-Distance estimator}
\label{sec:fundwise_lmd_estimator}



In this subsection, we define a generalized fund-level criterion that averages pricing errors across a user-specified set of discounting dates $\mathcal{T}_i$. 
This nests the inception-only NPV criterion of \cite{DLP12} as a special case and allows us to study how horizon averaging trades structural validity against finite-sample regularization. 
Throughout, only pooled fund cash flows are observed, whereas the deal-level decomposition from Section~\ref{sec:asset_pricing_pooled} remains a latent theoretical device.

The stochastic discount factor $\Psi_{\tau,t} = \Psi_{t} / \Psi_{\tau}$ is used to calculate the time-$\tau$ price $P_{\tau,t,i}$ of a \textbf{single} time-$t$ cash flow of any given PE fund $i$
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:price}
P_{\tau,t,i} := \Psi_{\tau,t} \cdot CF_{i,t}
= \frac{\Psi_{t}}{\Psi_{\tau}} \cdot CF_{i,t}
\qquad \forall \quad \tau,t,i
\end{equation}
with again multi-period SDF $\Psi_t = \prod_{u=1}^t M_u$ and $\Psi_0 = 1$.
As SDFs are commonly parameterized by a vector $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^{p}$, i.e., $\Psi_{\tau,t} \equiv \Psi_{\tau,t} (\theta)$, our goal is to find an estimation method for the true parameter vector $\theta_0$.

For each fund $i$ and all points $\tau$ within a common fund lifetime, the empirical pricing error $\epsilon_{\tau,i}(\theta)$ of \textbf{all} realized-to-date fund cash flows is calculated as a time-$\tau$ revaluation of the entire cash-flow stream
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pricing_error}
\epsilon_{\tau,i}(\theta) := \sum_{t=0}^T P_{\tau,t,i}(\theta) 
\qquad \forall \quad \tau,i
\end{equation}
Note that for $t<\tau$, the terms $\frac{\Psi_t}{\Psi_\tau}CF_{i,t}$ are compounded past cash flows, not discounted future cash flows, so the term ``present value'' is no longer accurate for $\tau > t_i^0$. For $\tau>t_i^0$, $\epsilon_{\tau,i}(\theta)$ is not a continuation value but a time-$\tau$ revaluation of the entire realized cash-flow stream, and therefore generally inherits the Compounding-Horizon Wedge characterized in Section~\ref{sec:asset_pricing_pooled}.
This time-$\tau$ revaluation $\epsilon_{\tau,i}(\theta)$ must be sharply distinguished from the latent continuation value ${LCV}_{i,\tau}(\theta) := \mathbb{E}[\sum_{t=\tau}^{T}(\Psi_t/\Psi_\tau)CF_{i,t} \mid \mathcal{F}_\tau]$, which conditions on information at $\tau$ and is not directly observed.

To analyze the effect of different discounting dates $\tau$ on the bias and variance of the estimated SDF parameters, we define the generalized criterion $\bar{\epsilon}_{i}(\theta)$ that averages pricing errors over the set $\mathcal{T}_i$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:average_pricing_error}
\bar{\epsilon}_{i}(\theta) =
w_{i} \cdot
\frac{1}{ \mathrm{card}(\mathcal{T}_{i}) }
\sum_{\tau \in \mathcal{T}_{i}}
\epsilon_{\tau,i}(\theta)
\qquad \forall \quad i
\end{equation}
where $w_i$ normalizes fund-level pricing errors before the loss is applied; it is not an observation weight in the outer sample average. Depending on the application, $w_i$ may normalize by invested capital, by aggregate vintage-year capital, by a macroeconomic deflator, or be set to one when cash flows are already expressed in comparable scale units.

\paragraph{The role of $\mathcal{T}_i$}

The set $\mathcal{T}_i$ controls which discounting dates enter the averaged pricing error $\bar{\epsilon}_i(\theta)$ in Equation~\eqref{eq:average_pricing_error} (see Figure~\ref{fig:npvs}). 
Averaging over multiple dates in $\mathcal{T}_i$ was originally motivated by the ``exploding alpha'' problem described by \cite{DLP12}: when the initial cash flow is very small, an arbitrarily large alpha can discount all subsequent cash flows close to zero, rendering the beta factors irrelevant and distorting estimation. 
Thus, their inception-only objective function is not globally convex in finite samples: the multiplicative SDF parameterization permits extreme parameter regions in which the discount factors drive all $NPV_i$ close to zero, creating spurious near-minima that can prevent reliable identification of $\alpha$.
Averaging over $\mathcal{T}_i$ mitigates this degeneracy (see Figure~\ref{fig:exploding_alpha}).
The relevant horizon family is parameterized as
\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:t_cal_horizon}
    \mathcal{T}_{i,H} = \left\{t_i^0,t_i^0+1,\dots,\min(t_i^0+12H,T_i^{\max})\right\}, \qquad H\ge 0.
\end{equation}

This formulation encompasses two economically distinct cases:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $H=0$: If $\mathcal{T}_i = \{t_i^0\}$, the estimator uses an inception-only NPV criterion, is anchored in the pooled pricing restriction from Section~\ref{sec:asset_pricing_pooled}, and targets the structural parameter $\theta_0$.
    \item $H>0$: If $\mathcal{T}_i = \mathcal{T}_{i,H}$ with $H>0$, the estimator is a generalized horizon-averaged criterion and targets the pseudo-true parameter $\theta_H^{\dagger}$. It acts as a finite-sample regularization device by smoothing the sample criterion, but it does so by introducing the Compounding-Horizon Wedge and therefore changing the estimand. 
\end{itemize}
Larger values of the horizon $H$ offer more regularization, but a larger departure from the structural benchmark with zero expected value.

For $\mathcal{T}_{i,H}=\{t_i^0,t_i^0+1,\dots,\min(t_i^0+12H,T_i^{\max})\}$, the estimator targets the horizon-specific pseudo-true parameter $\theta_H^{\dagger}$, which coincides with the structural parameter $\theta_0$ only when $H=0$. For $H>0$, the estimator trades structural fidelity against finite-sample regularization by averaging pricing errors over discounting dates that may incorporate the Compounding-Horizon Wedge from Section~\ref{sec:asset_pricing_pooled}.
Online Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_bias_variance} analyzes this target shift mechanism in rigorous detail, providing an exact Mean Squared Error (MSE) decomposition that mathematically formalizes how $\mathcal{T}$-averaging dampens finite-sample criterion volatility at the cost of shifting the population pseudo-true target point.

\paragraph{Restriction used for identification and asymptotic theory.} 
In the consistency analysis below, we specialize the generalized criterion to $\mathcal{T}_i = \{t_i^0\}$. Under Proposition~\ref{theo:inception_value}, this yields a valid pooled fund-level zero-moment restriction at the structural parameter $\theta_0$.
This restriction guarantees that $\mathbb{E}[\bar{\epsilon}_i(\theta_0)] = 0$ under the true parameter.
For the quadratic loss $L(x) = x^2$, the population objective $Q_0(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}[\bar{\epsilon}_i(\theta)^2] = -\mathrm{Var}[\bar{\epsilon}_i(\theta)] - (\mathbb{E}[\bar{\epsilon}_i(\theta)])^2$ involves both variance and bias terms, so consistency requires that $\theta_0$ uniquely maximizes this composite criterion (see Proposition~\ref{prop:consistency_H}).

\paragraph{LMD estimator.} 
Because our estimator is defined through the optimization of a sample criterion, it is an extremum estimator in the sense of \citet[Eq.~1.1]{NM94}, with $\hat{\theta} = \arg\max_{\theta \in \Theta} Q_n(\theta)$.
Concretely, our Least Mean Distance (LMD) estimator \cite[Equation 7.1]{PP97} minimizes the average loss of $\bar{\epsilon}_{i}(\theta)$.
For consistency with the extremum-estimator convention, we will technically maximize the average negative loss of $\bar{\epsilon}_{i}(\theta)$, which is equivalent.

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:estimator}
\hat{\theta} = 
\arg \min_{\theta \in \Theta}
\enspace
\frac{1}{n}
\sum_{i=1}^n
L \bigl( \bar{\epsilon}_{i}(\theta) \bigr) 
\equiv
\arg \max_{\theta \in \Theta}
\enspace
Q_n(\theta)
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation*}
Q_n(\theta) = -
\frac{1}{n}
\sum_{i=1}^n
L \bigl( \bar{\epsilon}_{i}(\theta) \bigr) 
\end{equation*}
where $L: \mathbb{R}^{1} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^1$ denotes a loss function, e.g., $L(x)=(x-0)^2$ in the case of nonlinear least squares.
Throughout the paper, the weighted average fund pricing error $\bar{\epsilon}_i \equiv \bar{\epsilon}_i(\theta)$ is regarded as a nonlinear random function of the SDF parameter $\theta$.




\subsection{Asymptotic benchmark and SHAC inference}
\label{sec:asymptotic_framework}

The asymptotic theory plays a supporting role in the paper. It provides a large-sample benchmark for the horizon-indexed criterion actually being estimated. It also clarifies the population object associated with each horizon and motivates the dependence-robust covariance estimator used later in the empirical analysis. Because the available private-equity panels remain small and heavily overlapping, we do not treat asymptotic approximations as the primary source of empirical credibility in finite samples.

The large-sample analysis is stated for the horizon-indexed estimator
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:estimator_H}
\hat{\theta}_H
=
\arg\max_{\theta \in \Theta} Q_{n,H}(\theta),
\qquad
Q_{n,H}(\theta)
:=
-
\frac{1}{n}
\sum_{i=1}^n
L\!\left(
\bar{\epsilon}^{(H)}_i(\theta)
\right),
\end{equation}
where \(\bar{\epsilon}^{(H)}_i(\theta)\) denotes the horizon-averaged pricing error based on \(\mathcal T_{i,H}\) from Section~\ref{sec:fundwise_lmd_estimator}. Throughout this subsection, the horizon parameter \(H \ge 0\) is fixed while \(n \to \infty\). The notation makes explicit that both the estimator and its probability limit generally depend on \(H\).

Define the horizon-specific population objective by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:population_criterion_H}
Q_H(\theta)
:=
\mathbb E\!\left[Q_{n,H}(\theta)\right]
=
-
\mathbb E\!\left[
L\!\left(
\bar{\epsilon}^{(H)}_i(\theta)
\right)
\right],
\end{equation}
and let the corresponding population target be
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pseudotrue_target}
\theta_H^\dagger
:=
\arg\max_{\theta \in \Theta} Q_H(\theta).
\end{equation}

The asymptotic benchmark is intentionally formulated around \(\theta_H^\dagger\), not around \(\theta_0\). For \(H=0\), the pooled inception-only criterion coincides with the structural pricing restriction of Section~\ref{sec:asset_pricing_pooled}; the benchmark target is structural when the inception-only population criterion is uniquely maximized at \(\theta_0\). For \(H>0\), the criterion generally targets a pseudo-true parameter \(\theta_H^\dagger \neq \theta_0\), because averaging over \(\tau>t_i^0\) may embed the Compounding-Horizon Wedge at the population level.

We use increasing-domain asymptotics indexed by vintage years, with local overlap dependence and bounded within-vintage clustering; the full regularity conditions are stated in Online Appendix~\ref{sec:proofs_asymptotics_H}. 
Under those conditions, the estimator fits the semiparametric extremum framework of \citet{PP97} and \citet{NM94}, while the required uniform law of large numbers and random-field central limit theorem follow from \citet{JP09,JP12}.

\begin{proposition}[Consistency around the horizon-specific target]
\label{prop:consistency_H}
Under the regularity conditions stated in Online Appendix~\ref{sec:proofs_asymptotics_H},
\[
\hat{\theta}_H
\xrightarrow{p}
\theta_H^\dagger
\qquad \text{as } n \to \infty.
\]
\end{proposition}

This is the horizon-indexed analogue of the standard extremum-estimator consistency result of \citet{NM94}. For \(H=0\), the probability limit coincides with the structural parameter \(\theta_0\) when the inception-only population criterion is uniquely maximized at \(\theta_0\); for \(H>0\), consistency is around the horizon-specific pseudo-target \(\theta_H^\dagger\).

\begin{proposition}[Asymptotic normality around the horizon-specific target]
\label{prop:clt_H}
Under the regularity conditions stated in Online Appendix~\ref{sec:proofs_asymptotics_H},
\[
\sqrt{n}
\left(
\hat{\theta}_H - \theta_H^\dagger
\right)
\overset{d}{\longrightarrow}
\mathcal N(0,\Sigma_H),
\qquad
\Sigma_H
=
\mathcal{H}_H^{-1}\Lambda_H \mathcal{H}_H^{-1}.
\]
\end{proposition}

This combines the extremum-estimator expansion of \citet{NM94} with the increasing-domain random-field CLT of \citet{JP12}. 
As with consistency, the special case \(H=0\) is centered at the structural parameter \(\theta_0\) only under the additional structural-identification condition that the inception-only population criterion is uniquely maximized at \(\theta_0\); for \(H>0\) the asymptotic center is the pseudo-target \(\theta_H^\dagger\).

For large-sample inference, let \(s_i^{(H)}(\theta):=\nabla_\theta L(\bar{\epsilon}^{(H)}_i(\theta))\) denote the score contribution and \(\hat{\mathcal{H}}_H:=\nabla_{\theta\theta} Q_{n,H}(\hat\theta_H)\) the sample Hessian.
We estimate the long-run score covariance with the spatial HAC estimator of \citet{KS11} adapted to the vintage-year metric:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:shac_H}
\hat{\Lambda}_H
=
\frac{1}{n}
\sum_{i=1}^n
\sum_{j=1}^n
k_{i,j}
\,
s_i^{(H)}(\hat\theta_H)
s_j^{(H)}(\hat\theta_H)^\top,
\qquad
k_{i,j}
=
K\!\left(
\frac{d_{i,j}}{b_V}
\right),
\end{equation}
where \(d_{i,j}\) is the vintage-year distance metric and \(b_V\) is the SHAC bandwidth; the full assumptions and implementation details are given in Online Appendix~\ref{sec:proofs_asymptotics_H}. 
The resulting asymptotic covariance matrix is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:avar_H}
\widehat{\mathrm{Avar}}(\hat\theta_H)
=
\frac{1}{n}
\hat{\mathcal{H}}_H^{-1}
\hat{\Lambda}_H
\hat{\mathcal{H}}_H^{-1}.
\end{equation}
Asymptotic standard errors then follow in the usual way $\mathrm{SE}(\hat\theta_H)=\sqrt{\mathrm{diag}\left[\widehat{\mathrm{Avar}}(\hat\theta_H)\right]}$.



When \(H>0\), inference based on Equation~\eqref{eq:avar_H} pertains to the pseudo-true parameter \(\theta_H^\dagger\); it therefore serves as a large-sample benchmark for stability diagnostics and model comparison, not as a direct structural test about \(\theta_0\).

Even when finite-sample inference is weak, this asymptotic benchmark still matters for interpretation. It defines the population object associated with each horizon-indexed criterion, makes the distinction between the structural parameter \(\theta_0\) and the horizon-specific pseudo-target \(\theta_H^\dagger\) precise, and motivates the SHAC covariance estimator as the appropriate large-sample reference under overlap dependence. In the empirical sections, we use this benchmark to interpret, rather than override, the simulation and cross-validation evidence.



\subsection{Comparison to similar estimators}
\label{sec:comparison_to_similar_estimators}

Our Least-Mean-Distance (LMD) estimator belongs to the broad class of semiparametric nonlinear extremum estimators in the sense of \citet{PP97}. 
Its main advantage in the present setting is that it accommodates pooled fund- or portfolio-level pricing errors, flexible horizon averaging through \(\mathcal T_{i,H}\), and increasing-domain asymptotics indexed by vintage years, all within a comparatively transparent objective function.

This section compares it to three nearby contributions, each along a different margin.
Relative to \citet{DLP12}, our criterion nests the inception-only objective exactly, but relies on a different asymptotic device. 
Relative to \cite{DLP09wp}, our horizon averaging addresses the same stabilization problem as ratio or log-ratio transformations, but changes the bias channel differently. 
Relative to \citet{KN16}, we share the discounted-cash-flow pricing logic, but differ in aggregation and in the asymptotic source of identifying variation.

\subsubsection{Connection to \cite{DLP12}}

The connection to \citet{DLP12} is the closest at the objective-function level.
Under the inception-only restriction, equal normalization, and quadratic loss, the LMD criterion coincides with the identity-weighted one-step cross-sectional GMM objective used in their framework.
Recall that \citet{DLP12} estimate $(\hat\alpha,\hat\beta) = \arg\min_{\alpha,\beta}\sum_{i=1}^n [NPV_i(\alpha,\beta)]^2$, where $NPV_i$ discounts all cash flows of portfolio~$i$ to fund inception.
To bridge the gap, let us rewrite $NPV_i$ in our more general SDF notation as $PV_i (\theta) = \sum_{t \in \left\{ t_i^0, t_i^0 + 1, \dots \right\}} \Psi_{t_i^0, t}(\theta) CF_{i,t}$, where $t_i^0$ denotes the fund inception date as defined in Section~\ref{sec:asset_pricing_pooled}.

\begin{proposition}[Connection to the \cite{DLP12} objective]
\label{prop:dlp12_connection}
Suppose that:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \(\mathcal T_i = \{t_i^0\}\) for all \(i\),
    \item \(w_i = 1\) for all \(i\),
    \item \(L(x)=x^2\),
    \item unit \(i\) is a vintage-year portfolio.
\end{enumerate}
Then the LMD estimator solves
\[
\hat{\theta}
=
\arg\min_{\theta \in \Theta}
\frac{1}{n}
\sum_{i=1}^n
\left(
PV_i(\theta)
\right)^2,
\]
which is equivalent to the identity-weighted cross-sectional GMM objective in \citet{DLP12}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Under \(\mathcal T_i = \{t_i^0\}\), the averaged pricing error reduces to the inception-based pooled net present value
\[
\bar{\epsilon}_i(\theta)
=
\epsilon_{t_i^0,i}(\theta)
=
\sum_{t=0}^T
\frac{\Psi_t(\theta)}{\Psi_{t_i^0}(\theta)}
CF_{i,t}
=
PV_i(\theta).
\]
With \(w_i=1\) and \(L(x)=x^2\), Equation~\eqref{eq:estimator} becomes
\[
\hat{\theta}
=
\arg\min_{\theta \in \Theta}
\frac{1}{n}
\sum_{i=1}^n
\left(
PV_i(\theta)
\right)^2.
\]
Writing the vector of pooled pricing errors as \(e(\theta) = (PV_1(\theta),\dots,PV_n(\theta))^\top\), the objective is proportional to \(e(\theta)^\top e(\theta)\), which is the identity-weighted one-step GMM criterion.
\end{proof}

\citet{DLP12} justify this inception-based estimator under a different asymptotic device than the one used here. 
Their Theorem~1 fixes the number of vintage-year portfolios and lets the number of underlying projects or funds within each portfolio diverge, yielding consistency for the inception-only estimator under their maintained model \citep[p.\ 520]{DLP12}. 
Economically, this portfolio-infill asymptotic sequence averages out within-portfolio idiosyncratic noise, but it does not by itself create new realizations of aggregate states. 
Relative to that setup, our framework preserves the exact \(H=0\) nesting result while letting the vintage-year domain itself expand, which is better aligned with unconditional identification, horizon comparisons, and overlap dependence when new vintages bring new realizations of macroeconomic conditions.

\subsubsection{Connection to \cite{DLP09wp}}

The earlier working-paper version, \cite{DLP09wp}, provides the closest conceptual precursor to our $NFV$ approach. 
Starting from the same inception-based $NPV$ criterion as \cite{DLP12}, they show that the raw sum of squared $NPV$s is not globally convex in finite samples (the ``exploding alpha'' problem mentioned before) and stabilize estimation by transforming the $NPV$ relation itself. 
Their two proposed ``GMM-style'' criteria are a log-transformed specification based on the difference between the log present values of investments and dividends and a PME-style ratio specification akin to \cite{KS05} that minimizes the distance between the ratio of these present values and one. 
This is the main conceptual connection to our framework: both approaches use transformed-\(NPV\) criteria to obtain a better-behaved objective, but the source of the resulting bias is different.

To make the asymptotic logic explicit without introducing additional counting notation, let \(\overline{PV}^{D}(\theta)\) and \(\overline{PV}^{I}(\theta)\) denote the discounted portfolio averages of dividends and investments within a generic vintage-year portfolio, and let \(\overline{NPV}(\theta)=\overline{PV}^{D}(\theta)-\overline{PV}^{I}(\theta)\). 
Under the working paper's asymptotic scheme, the number of funds inside each fixed vintage-year portfolio diverges, so the PME transformation inherits the same population zero set as the inception-\(NPV\) criterion:
\[
\frac{\mathbb{E}[\overline{PV}^{D}(\theta_0)]}{\mathbb{E}[\overline{PV}^{I}(\theta_0)]} - 1
=
\frac{\mathbb{E}[\overline{NPV}(\theta_0)]}{\mathbb{E}[\overline{PV}^{I}(\theta_0)]}.
\]
Hence, when \(\mathbb{E}[\overline{NPV}(\theta_0)] = 0\) and \(\mathbb{E}[\overline{PV}^{I}(\theta_0)]>0\), the transformed and untransformed portfolio criteria are asymptotically equivalent. 
The cost of stabilization is therefore not a different asymptotic target, but a finite-sample nonlinearity bias: \cite{DLP09wp} find a downward bias for \(\alpha\) under the log transformation and an upward bias under the ratio specification.

The contrast to our framework is that their transformation is applied after within-portfolio averaging. 
If the ratio transformation were treated as primitive at the unit level before aggregation, the target would change. For a generic unit \(i\) with \(NPV_i(\theta)=PV_i^D(\theta)-PV_i^I(\theta)\), one obtains at \(\theta_0\)
\begin{equation*}
	\mathbb{E}\!\left[\frac{PV_i^D(\theta_0)}{PV_i^I(\theta_0)}\right]
	- 1
	=
	\mathbb{E}\!\left[\frac{NPV_i(\theta_0)}{PV_i^I(\theta_0)}\right]
	=
	\mathrm{Cov}\!\left(
	NPV_i(\theta_0),
	\frac{1}{PV_i^I(\theta_0)}
	\right),
\end{equation*}
where the last equality uses \(\mathbb{E}[NPV_i(\theta_0)] = 0\). 
This expectation of a random ratio is not the ratio-of-expectations moment justified by \cite{DLP09wp}. 
The \cite{DLP09wp} construction therefore stabilizes estimation while preserving the same large-sample target under portfolio-infill asymptotics, whereas in our setup changing \(H\) changes the set of discounting dates and hence the population criterion itself. 
Different horizons can therefore target different pseudo-true parameters \(\theta_H^\dagger\) even asymptotically.
A practical advantage of our horizon-based approach is that it operates directly on net cash-flow streams. 
The two alternatives in \citet{DLP09wp}, by contrast, require separately observed and accurately labeled investment and dividend cash flows.



\subsubsection{Connection to \cite{KN16}}

Our framework is close to \citet{KN16} at the level of primitive discounted cash-flow moments, but it does not literally nest their estimator. 
If the cross-sectional unit is allowed to be an arbitrary pooled portfolio or calendar-time window, our notation can represent the same private-equity cash-flow moment building blocks that enter their GMM setup. 
In that sense, the connection is economically close.

The main difference is the sample objective. 
Our LMD estimator applies the loss before aggregation, minimizing an average of unit-level losses, whereas \citet{KN16} apply a GMM quadratic form to an aggregated mean moment vector. 
While both approaches target the true parameter $\theta_0$ under correct model specification, this distinction becomes economically meaningful under misspecification.
Because the \emph{average of losses} applies a different implicit asset weighting than the \emph{loss of an average}, the two approaches will generally converge to different pseudo-true parameter vectors when the model is misspecified, a pervasive feature of asset pricing models. 
In addition, their empirical implementation assigns zero weight to the private-equity pricing-error block during estimation, so the SDF is pinned down by public-equity and T-bill moments and the PE pricing error is evaluated only afterwards. 
Accordingly, \citet{KN16} are best viewed as a closely related aggregated-moment GMM analogue rather than as a special case of Equation~\eqref{eq:estimator}.

The asymptotic difference mirrors this design choice. 
The \citet{KN16} approach is natural when identification comes from repeated aggregate-state realizations: enlarging the cross section within a common payoff window averages out idiosyncratic fund noise, but not the common SDF realization itself. 
Their asymptotic logic therefore relies on additional nonoverlapping payoff windows rather than on cross-sectional replication alone \citep[p.\ 1447]{KN16}. 
Our framework instead uses increasing-domain asymptotics indexed by vintage years, with bounded within-vintage counts and bounded fund lifetimes, so that new vintages contribute new macroeconomic realizations while dependence remains local.

\subsubsection{Positioning of the present estimator}

The present estimator is best viewed as a synthesis tailored to the current application.
It combines the exact inception-only objective link to \citet{DLP12}, the stabilization concern emphasized in \cite{DLP09wp}, and the \citet{KN16} insight that identification requires new aggregate-state realizations rather than cross-sectional replication alone.
Operationally, these elements enter a pooled-unit nonlinear least-squares framework with explicit horizon control.

The practical distinction is which form of sample expansion supplies new identifying information.
If a researcher mainly gains more funds inside already-observed vintages, the \citet{DLP12} infill logic is the natural approximation. 
If the sample mainly lengthens in calendar time and thereby adds nonoverlapping payoff windows, the \citet{KN16} logic is closer to the underlying information structure. 
If the panel broadens chiefly by observing additional vintages while per-vintage counts remain modest, our increasing-domain framework is the more natural approximation.

\section{Implementation choices and simulation}
\label{sec:empirical_application}

In this section and the next, we move from the general methodology to our empirical application.
First, we describe the data and establish our sample restriction based on terminal cash-flow measurement concerns.
Second, we detail the empirical implementation choices that anchor the general estimator from Section~\ref{sec:fundwise_lmd_estimator}.
Third, we use a simulation study to validate and calibrate these choices.
Finally, Section~\ref{sec:empirical_results_bovc} presents the resulting empirical estimates for Buyout and Venture Capital funds.


\subsection{Private data}
\label{sec:data}

We use the Preqin cash flow dataset as of 14th April 2022, which is well known in the academic private equity literature \citep{HJK14,brown2015different,KN16,ACGP18}.
To keep the empirical analysis clear and concise, we filter the dataset for Buyout (BO) and Venture Capital (VC) funds.
We only keep funds with fund currency US Dollar (USD) and region equal to North America.

For this subset of BO and VC funds, we extract all (i) equal-weighted (hereafter EW) and (ii) value-weighted (hereafter FW, weighted by fund size) cash flow series (cf.\ Figures \ref{fig:preqin_cashflows_bo} and \ref{fig:preqin_cashflows_vc}).
In the overall dataset, the minimum vintage year is 1982, and the maximum is 2021.
We initially retain all vintage years, including recent ones.
Section~\ref{sec:empirical_vintage_cutoffs_bovc} assesses the impact of unseasoned fund Net Asset Values (NAVs) on our empirical estimation results.

Typical PE funds call approximately 33\% of their total capital commitments by year one, 50\% by year two, 66\% by year three, 80\% by year four, 88\% by year five, and 93\% by year six. 
These cumulative drawdown dynamics clarify why short compounding horizons come with smaller structural target shifts. 
Because investments initiated \emph{after} the maximum evaluation date in \(\mathcal{T}_{i,H}\) are priced strictly as future projects (\(\tau < t_{i,j}^{\mathrm{Inv}}\)), their conditional expected pricing error remains zero. 
Therefore, they are mathematically immune to the Compounding-Horizon Wedge. 
For example, under a compounding horizon of $H=4$ years, roughly 20\% of the aggregate investment cash flows occur \emph{after} the active averaging window. 
Thus, for $H=4$ roughly 20\% of the fund's capital is discounted without accumulating the covariance wedge that structurally drives the target shift.

\subsection{SDF models and public data}
\label{sec:model_selection}

We use as our baseline main-text specification a simple linear SDF model similar to \cite{DLP12}
\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:linear_sdf}
	\Psi_{\tau,t}^{\mathrm{SL}}(\theta)=
	\frac{\prod_{h=1}^{\tau}\left(1+\alpha+r_{f,h}+\sum_m \beta_m F_{m,h}\right)}
	     {\prod_{h=1}^{t}\left(1+\alpha+r_{f,h}+\sum_m \beta_m F_{m,h}\right)}
\end{equation}
with monthly (arithmetic) risk-free return $r_f$, monthly (arithmetic) zero-net-investment portfolio returns $F_m$, and parameter vector $\theta=(\alpha,\beta)$.
Throughout both the simulation and the empirical application, all factor returns and fund cash flows are observed at monthly frequency.

The public market factors that enter our SDF draw on the US dataset of the recently popularized $q^5$ investment factor model sourced from \url{http://global-q.org/factors.html} \citep{HXZ15,hou2021augmented}. 
According to \cite{hou2019factors} this family of new "investment" factors subsumes the more popular Fama-French factors \citep{fama2015five,fama2018choosing}, which indicates higher explanatory power of the \cite{hou2021augmented} model.
The $q^5$ five-factor model includes the market excess return (MKT), a size factor (ME), an investment factor (IA), a return on equity factor (ROE), and an expected growth factor (EG).
The maximum date in the  $q^5$ investment factor dataset is 2024-12-31.

To avoid overfitting, we restrict our analysis to six simple SDF models that contain \{MKT\} alone or \{MKT\} plus \{ME or IA or ROE or EG or Alpha\}.
In Equation~\eqref{eq:estimator}, we use the quadratic loss function $L(x)=x^2$.


In our optimization procedure, we limit the alpha intercept $\alpha$ to the range $[-2\%, +2\%]$ per month to avoid the ``exploding alpha'' issue documented in \cite[Footnote~8]{DLP12}: when $\alpha$ is unrestricted, an arbitrarily large $\alpha$ value can almost perfectly price any cash flow stream (except its first component), rendering the beta risk factors irrelevant.


\subsection{The impact of unseasoned funds and reported NAVs}
\label{sec:empirical_vintage_cutoffs_bovc}

Before defining our baseline sample, we highlight a structural limitation of including recent vintages: treating the final reported NAV as a terminal cash flow substitutes an expected continuation value for a realized payoff, violating the actual-divestment requirement of Assumption~\ref{ass:deal_level_dgp}.
We mitigate this by imposing a strict maximum vintage-year cutoff to ensure the sample primarily consists of seasoned funds with minimal remaining NAVs.

Figure~\ref{fig:max_vintage_BOVC_MKT} presents the corresponding sensitivity analysis for BO and VC under fund-value weighting (FW).
In both sub-samples, the estimated market beta drifts upward monotonically as more recent vintages are included.
For BO, $\hat{\beta}_{\mathrm{MKT}}$ at the 0-year Horizon (see Remark~\ref{rem:horizon_def}) rises from approximately 1.23 with a 2011 cutoff to 2.42 with the full sample (2021), with most of the increase concentrated after 2016.
For VC, the FW estimate climbs from 1.57 to 2.33 over the same range.
Discounting all final NAVs by 50\% substantially flattens this trend: the BO FW range compresses to 1.09--1.65 and the VC FW range to 1.41--1.54, consistent with the view that the drift is driven by mechanical reliance on subjectively reported NAVs for young funds.
These findings underpin the sample restriction to the 2010 maximum vintage year used throughout.
Figure~\ref{fig:max_vintage_BOVC_MKT_alpha} confirms that unseasoned post-2016 vintages similarly inflate the estimated intercept $\alpha$ in two-factor models, an effect mitigated by the 50\% NAV discount.

\begin{remark}[Definition of Horizon]
	\label{rem:horizon_def}
	Throughout the empirical section, we use the term \emph{Horizon} $H$ to denote the maximum compounding horizon in years used in the set of discounting dates $\mathcal{T}$ (cf.\ Equation~\eqref{eq:t_cal_horizon}). 
	Formally, starting from the fund inception date $t_i^0$, estimating a model with a Horizon $H$ implies that the estimator minimizes the average pricing error over the set of discounting dates $\mathcal{T}_{i,H} = \{t_i^0, t_i^0+1, \dots, \min(t_i^0+12H, T_i^{\max}) \}$, formally denoted as $\bar{\epsilon}_{i}^{(H)}$.
	Note that the Horizon has as upper bound the max cash flow date per fund or portfolio in our empirical application.
	This Horizon in years serves as x-axis in all subsequent empirical and simulation charts.
\end{remark}


\subsection{NAV discount sensitivity analysis}
\label{sec:empirical_nav_discount_bovc}

To further quantify the sensitivity of factor loading estimates to terminal NAV treatment, we conduct a systematic NAV discount analysis on the seasoned sample (maximum vintage year 2010).
Figure~\ref{fig:nav_disc_BOVC_MKT} reports single-factor MKT estimates under five NAV treatments: full reported NAV, 25\%, 50\%, 75\% constant discounts, and a complete write-off.

For BO, $\hat{\beta}_{\mathrm{MKT}}$ decreases monotonically with the discount.
At the 0-year Horizon under EW, estimates range from 1.26 (no discount) to 0.94 (100\% write-off); under FW, from 1.20 to 0.98.
At the 15-year Horizon, the EW range is 0.81--0.55 and the FW range 0.84--0.62.
The single-factor market model remains economically plausible across all treatments.

For VC, the pattern is analogous but the sensitivity is more muted under EW (0-year range: 1.88--1.74) due to the higher baseline beta.
Under FW, the effect is more pronounced, particularly at longer Horizons (15-year range: 1.07--0.73).

\subsection{Simulation study}
\label{sec:simulation_study}


Our Monte Carlo experiments examine the finite-sample bias and variance of the estimator.\footnote{As with any simulation study, it investigates the ability to identify the assumed data generating process rather than validating the corresponding SDF model itself.}
Specifically, we investigate (i) the efficiency gains of vintage-year portfolios versus individual funds; (ii) the tradeoff between the time-series ($V$) and cross-sectional ($n/V$) dimensions; (iii) multivariate identification under idiosyncratic noise; and (iv) the optimal choice of the maximum compounding Horizon $\mathcal{T}$.

The baseline simulation uses historical $q$-investment factor paths from 1986 to 2005 and generates 20 funds per vintage year ($V{=}20$).
Subsequent paragraphs vary one margin at a time; full scenario definitions are in the online repository.
The ``extended vintage'' scenarios use vintages 1967--2005 ($V{=}39$).
Each fund contains 15 deals with equal investment amounts and one divestment cash flow.
Deals are entered within the first five years (discrete uniform) and held for one to ten years (uniform).
Returns follow the linear SDF of Equation~\eqref{eq:linear_sdf} with $\beta_{\mathrm{MKT}}{=}1$, augmented by a normal i.i.d.\ monthly error ($\sigma{=}0.2$, zero mean); negative returns exceeding $-100\%$ trigger default with zero exit.
Unlike the shifted lognormal error of \cite{DLP12}, which precludes returns below roughly $-99\%$, our specification admits full default; we analyze the remaining design divergence in Appendix~\ref{sec:dlp_simulation}.
We evaluate the estimator over Horizons from 0 to 30 years and simulate 1000 replications for both vintage-year and single-fund portfolios.


\paragraph{Cross-sectional unit $i$:}

As anticipated in Subsection~\ref{sec:asymptotic_framework}, vintage-year portfolios exhibit lower bias and variance than individual funds.
At a 15-year Horizon, the mean (standard deviation) of $\hat{\beta}_{\mathrm{MKT}}$ is 0.966 (0.188) for vintage-year portfolios versus 0.887 (0.359) for individual funds. 
Figure~\ref{fig:simulation_funds_vs_vyps} illustrates that this advantage extends across the full Horizon curve: single-fund estimates are severely upward-biased at short Horizons ($\hat{\beta}_{\mathrm{MKT}} \approx 1.5$) and deteriorate to negative values at long Horizons, whereas vintage-year portfolios remain in a narrow range around 1.0.

This bias--variance improvement comes at the cost of dramatically fewer cross-sectional units, weakening asymptotic approximations.
This constraint likely explains why \cite{KN16} use individual funds in their SHAC framework, exploiting a larger sample for smaller standard errors.

\paragraph{Varying vintages $V$ and portfolio sizes $n/V$:}

A key question is whether precision improves more from extending $V$ or from enlarging $n/V$.
For this comparison, we switch to a reference scenario with $V{=}20$, $n/V{=}40$ and compare it with four alternatives that vary $V$ and $n/V$ (see Figures~\ref{fig:sim_vary_vin_sizes_alpha}--\ref{fig:sim_vary_vin_sizes}).

Increasing $n/V$ is substantially more effective at suppressing variance than adding vintages.
At the 15-year Horizon, doubling $n/V$ from 10 to 20 over 39 years reduces $\sigma(\hat{\beta}_{\mathrm{MKT}})$ by roughly 22\% (0.218 to 0.169).
Shrinking $V$ from 39 to 10 years elevates variance only moderately for the early sub-sample (1986--1995, $\sigma \approx 0.194$), though the late sub-sample (1996--2005) exhibits considerably higher variance ($\sigma \approx 0.432$), reflecting macro-environment sensitivity.
This hierarchy is even more pronounced in the two-factor setting, where increasing $n/V$ aggressively dampens spurious $\alpha$ variance.
These findings provide an empirical rationale for the finite-sample superiority of \cite{DLP12} infill asymptotics (fixing $V$, $n_v \to \infty$) over purely time-series aggregation.

Figure~\ref{fig:sim_q_factors} reports two-factor simulations pairing MKT with each non-market $q^5$ factor (ME, IA, ROE, EG), with $\beta_{\mathrm{MKT}}{=}1$ and $\beta_{\mathrm{2nd}}{=}0.5$.

\paragraph{Size of set $\mathcal{T}$ (Maximum Compounding Horizon):}

A central finding concerns the choice of $\mathcal{T}$.
Theoretically, discounting exclusively to inception ($H{=}0$), i.e.\ the \cite{DLP12} framework, yields unbiased estimates under the exact-pricing restriction (Equation~\eqref{eq:deal_pricing}).
However, in the reported simulation designs, bias is minimized not at $H{=}0$ but around 12.5--15 years: at short Horizons, idiosyncratic noise interacts non-linearly with estimation, inducing severe factor-loading bias.
Averaging pricing errors over multiple dates acts as a finite-sample stabilization device, improving the mean estimate far more than it reduces standard errors, while introducing the target shift mentioned in Section~\ref{sec:fundwise_lmd_estimator}.
Our shorter-lifetime simulations indicate that the optimal horizon requires tailored calibration rather than a simple fund-life heuristic.
But, because the finite-sample gains from horizon averaging are DGP-dependent and come with the horizon-specific target shift, horizon choice should at present be viewed as a tuning decision guided by simulations, stability diagnostics, and economic judgment rather than by a fully developed optimality theory.
We further analyze these results theoretically in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_bias_variance}.

\paragraph{Simulation conclusion:}
To conclude, our simulation study supports several implementation choices used in the empirical application. 
First, vintage-year portfolio formation helps improve estimator precision relative to single-fund estimation. 
Second, in the reported designs, increasing the number of funds per vintage appears more effective in controlling estimator variance than increasing the number of vintages (for i.i.d.\ error terms). 
Third, averaging pricing errors over multiple dates materially reduces the short-Horizon bias of the inception-only criterion in the reported designs, even though Section~\ref{sec:asset_pricing_pooled} shows that this stabilization comes with a horizon-specific target shift.

Accordingly, in the empirical application we use vintage-year portfolios and the simple linear SDF as the baseline specification, emphasize one-factor MKT specifications, treat two-factor results as exploratory, and focus on Horizons around 12.5--15 years while also reporting sensitivity over a broader grid.


\subsection{Role of asymptotic inference}

We compute asymptotic standard errors using the SHAC framework of Subsection~\ref{sec:asymptotic_framework} with Bartlett kernel bandwidth $b_V{=}12$ years.
These provide the benchmark large-sample approximation.

Because private-equity samples remain small and model choice is often data-driven, we complement asymptotic inference with $hv$-block cross-validation \citep{R00}, a consistent model-selection criterion for dependent data.
Each fold partitions the data into three disjoint groups:
(i)~a \emph{validation set} ($v$-block) of three consecutive vintage years;
(ii)~a \emph{buffer zone} ($h$-block) of three vintage years on each side, discarded to break dependence from overlapping cash flows; and
(iii)~an \emph{estimation set} comprising all remaining vintage years.
This three-way split is repeated across rolling vintage-year windows (Table~\ref{tab:hv_block_cv}), with the number of active folds adapting dynamically to the maximum vintage-year cutoff (e.g., seven folds when the sample is truncated at 2010).

\begin{remark}[Cross-validation as stability diagnostic]
	\label{rem:cv_diagnostic}
	The $hv$-block cross-validation quantities reported throughout this paper serve as \textbf{stability diagnostics and model-selection criteria} rather than inferential standard errors.
	They summarize the fold-to-fold variation in parameter estimates under dependence-reducing sample splits and are informative about model sensitivity and effective parameter curvature, but they do not provide a valid null distribution for the full-sample estimator.
	For formal statistical inference (standard errors and hypothesis tests), we rely on the SHAC-based asymptotic benchmark of Section~\ref{sec:asymptotic_framework}.
	In practice, $hv$-block cross-validation complements the asymptotic analysis by (i) diagnosing model stability and out-of-sample performance without relying on asymptotics and (ii) flagging over-parameterized specifications that appear well-identified in-sample but collapse out-of-sample.
\end{remark}

\section{Empirical results for BO and VC data}
\label{sec:empirical_results_bovc}

This section presents the empirical SDF estimates for Buyout (BO) and Venture Capital (VC) funds and uses them to assess which specifications are stably identified in the current cash-flow panel.
As the simulation study in Subsection~\ref{sec:simulation_study} confirms, we use vintage-year portfolios (VYPs) to estimate simple linear SDF models for the BO and VC sub-samples individually.
We restrict the data to North American, USD-denominated funds from the Preqin dataset described in Subsection~\ref{sec:data}.
This restriction ensures consistency between fund cash flows and the U.S.\ $q$-investment factors used throughout the analysis.
Moreover, we only consider funds with a maximum vintage year of 2010, to reduce the reliance on unrealized NAVs (see Section~\ref{sec:empirical_vintage_cutoffs_bovc}).

We analyze BO and VC as two distinct asset classes with fundamentally different risk profiles and report both equal-weighted (EW) and value-weighted (FW) estimates across a grid of compounding Horizons.
Our baseline specification is the single-factor MKT model estimated on vintage-year portfolios.
Two-factor models that add an alpha intercept or a single $q$-investment factor serve as robustness and exploratory extensions.

For each model specification, we report asymptotic standard errors (SE$_A$) from the SHAC framework alongside $hv$-block cross-validation fold dispersions (SD$_{CV}$), defined as the standard deviation of parameter estimates across hold-out folds.
Throughout the empirical analysis, $t_A$ denotes the $t$-ratio based on asymptotic standard errors; $\mathrm{SR}_{CV}$ denotes the signed stability ratio $\mathrm{SR}_{CV} := \hat{\theta} / \mathrm{SD}_{CV}$, where \(\hat{\theta}\) is the corresponding full-sample estimate.
Because \(\mathrm{SR}_{CV}\) preserves the sign of \(\hat{\theta}\), negative values simply indicate negative estimated coefficients; its absolute value \(|\mathrm{SR}_{CV}|\) measures the coefficient magnitude relative to fold-to-fold dispersion.
We use \(\mathrm{SR}_{CV}\) descriptively rather than as a formal test statistic.
As discussed in Remark~\ref{rem:cv_diagnostic}, $\mathrm{SR}_{CV}$ complements asymptotic inference as a stability diagnostic.
We report asymptotic significance markers for completeness, but when SE$_A$ is erratic, boundary solutions arise, or the cross-validation diagnostics indicate instability, we do not treat isolated significant $t_A$ values as decisive.

All R code and data (including the previous simulations) are available in an online repository: \url{https://github.com/quant-unit/Fundwise_SDF/tree/master/r_project}.

For BO, the alpha term absorbs a substantial share of the variation that otherwise loads on the market factor: under EW, $\hat{\alpha}$ ranges from 0.003 to 0.006 per month (and -0.001 for 17.5~years) and the MKT coefficient oscillates between 0.35 and 1.01, with no stable convergence across Horizons.

For VC, the two-factor model exhibits a different but similarly unstable pattern.
At short Horizons (0 under EW), the alpha term reaches its upper bound ($\hat{\alpha} = 0.020$/month) and the MKT coefficient drops to approximately 0.47, resembling the BO behavior.
However, as the Horizon increases beyond 5~years, the MKT loading rises steadily to values exceeding 2.3, while the alpha estimate turns progressively negative, reaching $-0.009$/month at 30~years.
We interpret this reversal as further evidence that the two-parameter decomposition is highly sensitive to Horizon choice and sample composition in VC.
Under FW, the instability is somewhat attenuated ($\hat{\alpha}$ remains close to zero at short Horizons; $\hat{\beta}_{\mathrm{MKT}} \approx 1.74$), but the overall fragility persists, with asymptotic standard errors exceeding 10 for some Horizons.

The BO and VC two-factor results therefore reinforce the same conclusion: among the specifications considered here, single-factor MKT models are the most stable for private-equity SDF estimation in our sample.

\subsection{Robustness: two-factor $q^5$-factor models}
\label{sec:empirical_q_factors_bovc}

We now pair MKT with each of the four non-market $q^5$ factors (ME, IA, ROE, EG) in two-factor specifications estimated separately for BO and VC.
Because the detailed estimation tables contain extensive Horizon-by-Horizon results, we summarize only the main patterns visible in Figures~\ref{fig:empirical_BO_q_factors} and~\ref{fig:empirical_VC_q_factors}; the full tables are available in the accompanying online repository.

For BO, the qualitative message is similar across the four non-market factors.
Second-factor loadings are often positive at short Horizons, but with increasing Horizon they generally attenuate, fluctuate around zero, or change sign.
ME is the strongest partial exception, yet even there the loading is not stable across the full Horizon grid and weighting schemes.
By contrast, the MKT loading remains close to the corresponding single-factor estimate, so adding a second $q^5$ factor does not materially change the market-risk assessment.
At the same time, the asymptotic standard errors are frequently erratic and sometimes extremely large, which reinforces the limited reliability of asymptotic inference in this setting.

VC exhibits the same pattern more starkly.
Several second-factor loadings are very large at short Horizons, but they vary sharply with the compounding Horizon and often reverse sign at intermediate or long Horizons.
This degree of Horizon sensitivity closely matches the weak-identification patterns documented in the simulation study.
Across specifications, the resulting estimates are materially less stable and less economically interpretable than the single-factor MKT benchmark.

A few second-factor loadings attain conventional asymptotic significance at isolated Horizons, but those signals are neither persistent across the Horizon grid nor matched by comparably strong cross-validation evidence.
Overall, none of the four non-market $q^5$ factors yields a stable, economically interpretable loading across Horizons for either asset class.
We therefore do not view these two-factor specifications as providing evidence as stable as the single-factor MKT model in the present sample.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Our generalized Least-Mean-Distance framework provides a flexible way to study identification and estimation of Stochastic Discount Factors (SDFs) using non-traded cash flows.
Methodologically, the paper recasts semiparametric pooled-cash-flow pricing as a nonlinear LMD problem with flexible discounting dates, nests the inception-only criterion of \citet{DLP12}, and clarifies why closely related horizon-averaged procedures need not target the same population object.
The central theoretical message is that Horizon choice is part of the estimand: inception-only discounting preserves the structural pooled pricing restriction, whereas averaging pricing errors over multiple dates can improve finite-sample recoverability (through both lower dispersion and lower higher-order bias) while generally shifting the population target to a Horizon-specific pseudo-true parameter.
Our increasing-domain asymptotic framework for overlapping vintages provides a benchmark for this identification problem.
Taken together, the methodological results answer the paper's opening question: with pooled non-traded cash flows, what can be identified with stability depends jointly on factor specification, the cash-flow data-generating process, and the chosen compounding Horizon.
Our Monte Carlo results show that the tradeoff between structural fidelity and finite-sample recoverability is economically relevant in the reported designs, with short-Horizon criteria often substantially less stable than intermediate-Horizon averages, especially once abnormal performance ($\alpha$) is estimated jointly with market exposure.


In the private-equity application, the main empirical takeaway is diagnostic rather than definitive.
Current seasoned cash-flow panels appear informative about coarse market exposure in BO and VC portfolios, but much less informative about abnormal performance and richer factor decompositions. 
Across our specifications, the single-factor MKT model is the most stable, whereas adding $\alpha$ or a second non-market factor makes the estimates markedly more sensitive to Horizon choice and often weakens their economic interpretability.
We do not read this as evidence that private equity is priced by a single factor in population or that private-equity alpha is absent.
Rather, we read it as evidence that, under currently available pooled cash-flow data and semiparametric estimation, multi-parameter SDF models are only weakly identified in the present sample.
Accordingly, empirical practice in this setting should emphasize parsimonious specifications, robustness over a Horizon grid, and dependence-robust stability diagnostics such as $hv$-block cross-validation, while treating asymptotic significance as a useful benchmark rather than decisive finite-sample evidence.

More broadly, our results suggest that progress on richer private-equity SDF models will require either more informative data or additional structure.
Promising directions include better integration of interim valuation information and cash-flow dynamics, regularization or model-selection methods for sparse multi-factor settings, and principled procedures for choosing the set of discounting dates $\mathcal{T}$.
These extensions would help clarify when Horizon averaging improves finite-sample performance enough to justify the associated change in estimand.
